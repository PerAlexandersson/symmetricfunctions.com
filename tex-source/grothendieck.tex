\metatitle{Grothendieck polynomials}
\metadescription{An introduction to Grothendieck polynomials, including their definitions via divided difference operators and pipe dreams, stable Grothendieck polynomials and their properties such as Jacobi--Trudi identities, Pieri rules, Murnaghan--Nakayama rules, Schur expansions, and skew versions, as well as double Grothendieck polynomials and canonical stable Grothendieck polynomials.}

The Grothendieck polynomials are closely related to the \hyperref[schubertOperatorformula]{Schubert polynomials}.
They were introduced by \name{Alain Lascoux}, 1990 in \cite{Lascoux1990Grothendieck}.

\name{Neil J. Y. Fan} and \name{Peter L. Guo} give formulas for
Grothendieck polynomials as generating functions of set-valued Rothe tableaux
\cite{FanGuo2021Rothe}.  Their tableaux extend the usual Rothe-diagram
description of Schubert polynomials and include specializations for
vexillary, dominant, and Grassmannian permutations.


In \cite{BrubakerFrechetteHardtTiborWeber2020x}, the authors 
introduce biaxial Grothendieck polynomials, which generalize the double Grothendieck polynomials as well as 
the double dual Grothendieck polynomials.
They prove several results regarding Grothendieck polynomials, such as branching rules 
and Cauchy identities. They also provide a solvable lattice model whose partition function
is given by the Grothendieck polynomials, and duals.

\name{Harry Tamvakis} defines skew Grothendieck polynomials for skew elements
in Weyl groups of classical type and proves set-valued tableau formulas for
the skew double Grothendieck polynomials in types $A$, $B$, $C$, and $D$
\cite{Tamvakis2024}.  This also gives tableau formulas for Grassmannian
Grothendieck polynomials and $K$-theoretic skew Stanley functions in these
types.
\name{Eric Marberg} and \name{Brendan Pawlowski} study structural properties
of symplectic Grothendieck polynomials \cite{MarbergPawlowski2021}.  This
fits with the type $C$ side of $K$-theoretic Schubert calculus and the nearby
\hyperref[schurKPQ]{$K$-theoretic Schur $P$/$Q$} families.
\name{Eric Marberg} derives transition equations for classical double
Grothendieck polynomials, extending the type $A$ $K$-theoretic transition
relations to types $B$, $C$, and $D$ \cite{Marberg2025x}.  As an application,
the corresponding $K$-Stanley symmetric functions expand positively in
$K$-theoretic Schur $P$- and $Q$-functions.



\subsection[grothendieckOperatorformula]{Operator definition}

Define $\partial_i(f)$ as $\frac{f-s_i(f)}{x_i - x_{i+1}}$ for $i=1,\dotsc,n-1$,
and $\pi_i \coloneqq \partial_i(1-x_{i+1})$.

The Grothendieck polynomials are then defined as
\begin{equation*}
 \grothendieck_\omega(x_1,\dotsc,x_n) \coloneqq \pi_{\omega^{-1}\omega_0}(x_1^{n-1}x_2^{n-2}\dotsm x_{n-1}).
\end{equation*}

Note that the lowest degree homogeneous part of $\grothendieck_\omega(\xvec)$ is given by the Schubert polynomial
$\schubert_\omega(\xvec)$.


There is a connection with polytopes just as for Schubert polynomials, see \cite{MeszarosDizier2017}.


\subsection[grothendieckPipedreams]{Pipedreams}


As with Schubert polynomials, there is a way to describe 
Grothendieck polynomials using \hyperref[schubertPipeDream]{pipe dreams}, see \cite{FominKirillov1994grothendieck,KnutsonMiller2004}.
The double Grothendieck polynomials are given as
\[
\grothendieck^{(\beta)}_\omega(\xvec,\yvec) = 
\sum_{P \in PD(\omega)} 
\beta^{-\length(\omega)}
\prod_{(i,j)\in S(P)} \beta x_i \oplus y_j
\]
where we sum over \emph{pipe dreams}
and $a \oplus b = a+b+\beta ab$.
Here, $S(P)$ is the set of crosses the pipe dream (this particular version of the formula is from
\cite[Thm.6.1]{Weigandt2021}).



A more recent formula expresses the double Grothendieck polynomials 
as a sum over \emph{bumpless pipe dreams} \cite[Thm.1.1]{Weigandt2021}:
\[
\grothendieck^{(\beta)}_\omega(\xvec,\yvec) = 
\beta^{-\length(\omega)} 
\sum_{P \in BPD(\omega)} 
\prod_{(i,j)\in D(P)} \beta (x_i \oplus y_j )
\prod_{(i,j)\in NW(P)} 1+ \beta (x_i \oplus y_j )
\]
where $BPD(\omega)$ is the set of \defin{bumpless pipe dreams} of type $\omega$
and $NW(P)$ is the set of West-to-North pipe bends.

However, this formula does not immediately give the same pipe dream expression, 
as the Fomin--Kirillov formula, and it is an open problem 
to find a bijection between the two models.

A lattice model is also given in \cite{BuciumasScrimshaw2020x}, and a connection with bumpless pipe dreams is given.

\name[Karola Mészáros]{Karola Mészáros}, \name{Linus Setiabrata}, and
\name[Avery St. Dizier]{Avery St.~Dizier} give an orthodontia formula for
Grothendieck polynomials \cite{MeszarosSetiabrataDizier2020x}.  This extends
the orthodontia point of view from Schubert polynomials to the $K$-theoretic
setting.

\name{Chen-An Chou} and \name{Tianyi Yu} give an explicit algorithm for the
unique maximal pipe dream whose row and column weights are determined by the
$\mathsf{rajcode}$ statistic of \name{Oliver Pechenik},
\name{David Speyer}, and \name{Anna Weigandt} \cite{ChouYu2023x}.  Their
construction uses ladder moves and solves the direct-recipe problem for this
top-degree term of double Grothendieck polynomials.
\name{Chen-An Chou} and \name{Tianyi Yu} also study Grothendieck polynomials
of inverse fireworks permutations from the pipe-dream viewpoint
\cite{ChouYu2024x}.
\name{Chen-An Chou} and \name{Linus Setiabrata} prove saturated-Newton-polytope
results for fireworks Grothendieck polynomials \cite{ChouSetiabrata2025x}.
They characterize the support by divisibility between the Schubert support and
the maximal Grothendieck monomial, prove $M$-convexity after homogenization,
and compare supports with those of suitable layered permutations.

\name{Matt Dreyer}, \name[Karola Mészáros]{Karola Mészáros}, and
\name{Avery St. Dizier} give an alternative proof of the
Pechenik--Speyer--Weigandt degree formula for Grothendieck polynomials, using
the climbing-chain model \cite{DreyerMeszarosDizier2024}.  They also determine
the leading monomials of the homogeneous components for term orders with
$x_1 < x_2 < \dotsb < x_n$.
\name{Jenna Rajchgot}, \name{Yi Ren}, \name{Colleen Robichaux},
\name[Avery St. Dizier]{Avery St.~Dizier}, and \name{Anna Weigandt} relate
degrees of symmetric Grothendieck polynomials to Castelnuovo--Mumford
regularity \cite{RajchgotRenRobichauxDizierWeigandt2021}.
\name{Elena S. Hafner} studies supports of Castelnuovo--Mumford polynomials,
the top-degree components of Grothendieck polynomials
\cite{Hafner2026x}.  She proves the expected M-convex support property for
new families of permutations and studies when the support is the set of
integer points in a schubitope.

\name{Colleen Robichaux} gives a minimal counterexample in $\symS_8$ to the
Ross--Yong $K$-Kohnert conjecture for Grothendieck polynomials
\cite{Robichaux2024x}.  The same paper proposes a revised $K$-theoretic
Kohnert rule for Grothendieck polynomials and proves both the original and
revised rules in the $321$-avoiding case.

\name{Elena S. Hafner} studies vexillary Grothendieck polynomials through
bumpless pipe dreams \cite{Hafner2022x}.  She characterizes the bumpless pipe
dreams contributing maximal-degree terms and compares the
Pechenik--Speyer--Weigandt and Rajchgot--Robichaux--Weigandt degree formulas in
the vexillary case.
\name{Adam Gregory}, \name{Zachary Hamaker}, and \name{Tianyi Yu} give a
tableau formula for vexillary double $\beta$-Edelman--Greene coefficients
\cite{GregoryHamakerYu2024x}.  The formula is manifestly
$\beta$-Graham positive and refines known positivity for these coefficients.

\name{Oliver Pechenik} and \name{Matthew Satriano} prove a conjectured
Möbius-inversion formula for Grothendieck polynomials
\cite{PechenikSatriano2024Mobius}.  In the case where the corresponding
Schubert polynomial is a multiplicity-free monomial sum, the Grothendieck
polynomial is recovered from it by Möbius inversion on an associated poset.



\subsection[grothendieckASM]{Alternating sign matrices}


There is an expression for Grothendieck polynomials using 
alternating sign matrices, due to \name{Alain Lascoux}.

The \defin{stable Grothendieck polynomials} were introduced by \name{Sergey Fomin} and \name{Anatoly Kirillov} in \cite{FominKirillov1994grothendieck,FominKirillov1996YangBaxter}.
Similar to \hyperref[stanleySymLimitDefinition]{Stanley symmetric functions},
the stable Grothendieck polynomials are defined as the limit 
\begin{equation*}
 \grothendieckStable_\omega(\xvec) \coloneqq \lim_{k\to \infty}  \grothendieck_{1^k \times \omega}(\xvec).
\end{equation*}

\name{Damir Yeliussizov} studies positive specializations of symmetric
Grothendieck polynomials, giving a $K$-theoretic counterpart to the
Edrei--Thoma style parametrization for Schur-positive specializations
\cite{Yeliussizov2020Positive}.

\name{Jennifer Morse}, \name{Jianping Pan}, \name{Wencin Poh}, and
\name{Anne Schilling} construct a $K$-theoretic type $A$ crystal on
decreasing factorizations in the $0$-Hecke monoid \cite{MorsePanPohSchilling2020}.
Their insertion map relates this model to Hecke insertion, set-valued
tableaux, and uncrowding.

\name{Ben Brubaker}, \name[A. Suki Dasher]{A.~Suki Dasher},
\name{Michael Hu}, \name{Nupur Jain}, \name{Yifan Li}, \name{Yi Lin},
\name{Maria Mihaila}, \name{Van Tran}, and
\name[I. Deniz Unel]{I.~Deniz {\"U}nel} give a solvable lattice model for
twisted Kirillov polynomials obtained from universal divided-difference
operators \cite{BrubakerDasherHuJainLiLinMihailaTranUnel2026}.  Their model
specializes to Schubert, $\beta$-Grothendieck, dual $\beta$-Grothendieck, and
Di Francesco--Zinn-Justin polynomials, and proves Kirillov's nonnegativity
conjecture for the Hecke--Grothendieck specialization.

See \hyperref[grassmannianPermutation]{this page} for the definition of 
a Grassmann permutation, and how to associate a partition to such a permutation.
For a nice recent paper with lots of references and results, see \cite{MonicalPechenikScrimshaw2018}.


Stable Grothendieck polynomials indexed by Grassmann permutations are symmetric polynomials.
They serve as a K-theoretic analog of the \hyperref[stanleySym]{Stanley symmetric functions}.
These can be computed using a combinatorial formula, using set-valued tableaux:
\begin{align}
\grothendieckStable_\lambda(\xvec) = \sum_{T \in \mathrm{SVT}(\lambda)} (-1)^{|T|-|\lambda|} \xvec^T.
\end{align}
The set $\mathrm{SVT}(\lambda)$ consists of all set-valued tableaux of shape lambda,
where each box is filled with a non-empty set of natural numbers.
Rows must be weakly increasing and column must be strictly increasing.
The sum of the sizes of the sets in $T$ is denoted $|T|$.

A \hyperref[gtpatterns]{Gelfand--Tsetlin pattern} type combinatorial formula is described in \cite{MotegiSakai2014},
where marked GT-pattern are used to give the monomial expansion of $\grothendieckStable_\lambda$.
See also \cite{MonicalPechenikScrimshaw2018}, where they give an alternative proof of this.


\subsection[grothendieckStableWeyl]{Weyl identity}

A Weyl-type bialternant formula is given by the following quotient,
see \cite{IkedaNaruse2013,Kirillov2016,Iwao2019}.

\begin{align}
\grothendieckStable_\lambda(x_1,\dotsc,x_n) = 
\frac{ \det \left( x_i^{\lambda_j+n-j}(1+\beta x_i)^{j-1} \right)_{1\leq i,j \leq n} }{
\prod_{1 \leq i \lt j \leq n} (x_i - x_j)
} \qquad (\beta = -1).
\end{align}


\subsection[grothendieckStableJacobiTrudi]{Jacobi--Trudi identity}

There is a Jacobi--Trudi type identity, \cite[Thm. 1.10]{Kirillov2016} given by
\begin{align}
\grothendieckStable_\lambda(\xvec) = 
\det\left(
\sum_{m=0}^\infty \binom{i-1}{m} \beta^m \completeH_{\lambda_i-i+j+m}(\xvec)
\right)_{1\leq i,j \leq n}.
\end{align}


\subsection[grothendieckStablePieri]{Pieri rule}

\name{Cristian Lenart} \cite{Lenart2000} proved the following Pieri rule for stable 
Grassmann Grothendieck polynomials:
\[
\grothendieckStable_{(a)}\grothendieckStable_{\lambda} = 
\sum_{\mu / \lambda \text{ horizontal strip}} 
(-1)^{|\mu/\lambda|-a} \binom{ \textrm{rows}(\mu/\lambda)-1 }{ |\mu/\lambda|-a } \grothendieckStable_{\mu}
\]
where $\textrm{rows}(\mu/\lambda)$ is the number of rows in the horizontal strip.



\subsection[grothendieckStableMN]{Murnaghan--Nakayama rule}

In \cite{NguyenHiepSonThuy2023}, the following Murnaghan--Nakayama type rule is the main result.
\begin{align}
\grothendieckStable_\lambda(\xvec) \powerSum_k(\xvec) = 
 \sum_{\mu} (\beta)^{|\mu|-|\lambda|-k} (-1)^{k-c(\mu/\lambda)} \binom{r(\mu/\lambda)-1}{k-c(\mu/\lambda)} 
 \grothendieckStable_\mu(\xvec)
 \end{align}
where the sum runs over all connected skew shapes $\mu/\lambda$,
containing at least one $k$-ribbon along its northwestern border.
Here, $c(\mu/\lambda)$ is the number of columns spanned by the shape. Likewise,
$r(\mu/\lambda)$ is the number of rows spanned by the shape.

Note that at $\beta=0$, we recover the classical 
\hyperref[murnaghanNakayamaRule]{Murnaghan--Nakayama rule for Schur polynomials.}


\subsection[grothendieckStableSchurExpansion]{Schur expansion}

\name{Cristian Lenart} \cite[Thm. 2.8]{Lenart2000} prove that whenever $k\geq \length(\lambda)$,
\[
\grothendieckStable_{\lambda}(x_1,\dotsc,x_k)
= \sum_{\lambda \subseteq \mu \subseteq \hat{\lambda}} (-1)^{|\mu|-|\lambda|} a_{\lambda\mu} \schurS_{\mu}(\xvec)
\]
where the sum is taken over partitions $\mu$, and $\hat{\lambda}$ is the unique maximal partition of length $k$
obtained from $\lambda$ by adding at most $j-1$ boxes to the $j$-th row of the Young diagram $\lambda$.

The coefficients $a_{\lambda\mu}$ are non-negative, and 
count the number of \emph{increasing tableaux} of shape $\mu/\lambda$.
These are tableaux which are strictly increasing along rows and columns,
and for each $i\geq 1$, row $i$ only contain entries $\leq i-1$.


Alternatively, in \cite[Cor. 3.11]{MonicalPechenikScrimshaw2018}, it is proved that 
$a_{\lambda\mu}$ count the number of set-valued tableaux of shape $\lambda$ and weight $\mu$,
which are also Yamanouchi. The authors achieve this by
defining a type $A$ \hyperref[crystals]{crystal} graph on set valued fillings.


\subsection[grothendieckStableLittlewoodRichardson]{Littlewood--Richardson rule}

There is a Littlewood--Richardson rule for the product of stable Grothendieck polynomials:
\[
\grothendieckStable_{\lambda}(\xvec;\yvec) \grothendieckStable_{\mu}(\xvec;\yvec) = \sum_{\nu} 
(-1)^{|\lambda|+|\mu|-|\nu|} c_{\lambda\mu}^{\nu} \grothendieckStable_{\nu}(\xvec;\yvec)
\]
where $c_{\lambda\mu}^{\nu}$ is the number of set-valued tableaux of shape $\lambda * \mu$ with content $\nu$.

\name{Siddheswar Kundu} gives another model for these
$K$-theoretic Littlewood--Richardson coefficients using set-valued
contratableaux \cite{Kundu2025LR}.  This gives a tableau rule in the
same signed structure-constant setting as the stable Grothendieck product.



\subsection[grothendieckSkewStable]{Skew stable Grothendieck}


In \cite{Buch2002,LiMorseShields2016}, 
the skew version of the stable Grothendieck polynomials are defined as
\begin{align}
\grothendieckStable_{\nu/\lambda}(\xvec) = \sum_{\mu} 
\sum_{T \in \mathrm{SVT}(\nu/\lambda,\mu)} (-1)^{|\lambda|-|\nu|-|\mu|} \monomial_\mu(\xvec).
\end{align}

From a result in \cite{BilleyJockuschStanley1993}, it follows that the skew stable Grothendieck polynomials 
are precisely the stable limit of Grothendieck polynomials indexed by 321-avoiding permutations.


A recent result in \cite{ChanPflueger2019} gives an expansion of the skew stable Grothendieck polynomials
in terms of skew Schur polynomials. Here, $\sigma$ and $\mu$ are skew shapes.
\begin{align}
\grothendieckStable_{\sigma}(\xvec) = 
\sum_{\mu \supseteq \sigma} (-1)^{|B(\mu/\sigma)|} a_{\sigma,\mu} \schurS_\mu(\xvec).
\end{align}
The motivation behind this formula is the connection with Euler characteristics of certain varieties, see \cite{ChanPflueger2017}.

The coefficients $a_{\sigma,\mu}$ are non-negative integers with an explicit combinatorial interpretation.
\name{Melody Chan} and \name{Nathan Pflueger} also prove a similar \emph{inverse} formula, for connected skew shapes $\sigma$:
\begin{align}
\schurS_{\sigma}(\xvec) = 
\sum_{\mu \supseteq \sigma} (-1)^{|A(\mu/\sigma)|} b_{\sigma,\mu} \grothendieckStable_\mu(\xvec).
\end{align}

\name{Siddheswar Kundu} \cite{Kundu2024x} studies a flagged refined skew
stable Grothendieck polynomial $G_{\lambda/\mu}(X_\Phi;t)$.  Using a Demazure
crystal structure on flagged semistandard set-valued tableaux, he obtains an
expansion of this polynomial in key polynomials.  The coefficients carry the
usual K-theoretic signs, and at $t=0$ the formula recovers the
Reiner--Shimozono key expansion of flagged skew Schur functions.

\name{Peter L. Guo}, \name{Mingyang Kang}, and \name{Jiaji Liu} introduce
hybrid Grothendieck polynomials
$G_{\lambda/\mu}(\xvec;\tvec;\wvec)$ as generating functions over
set-valued reverse plane partitions \cite{GuoKangLiu2025x}.  They specialize
to refined stable Grothendieck and refined dual stable Grothendieck
polynomials, admit a Schur expansion from a crystal structure, and have
saturated Newton polytopes in straight shape.

\name{Shinsuke Iwao} gives a fermionic description of flagged skew
Grothendieck polynomials \cite{Iwao2023x}.  These are $K$-theoretic analogues
of flagged skew Schur polynomials, and the proof uses Matsumura's
Jacobi--Trudi type formula.



\subsection[grothendieckStableMoreInfo]{More information}


\name{Anders S. Buch}, \name{Andrew Kresch}, \name{Mark Shimozono},
\name{Harry Tamvakis}, and \name{Alexander Yong} describe the expansion of
stable Grothendieck polynomials into Grassmann Grothendieck polynomials
\cite{BuchKreschShimozonoTamvakisYong2007}.
\name{Tomoo Matsumura} expresses flagged Grothendieck polynomials, defined by
flagged set-valued tableaux, by a Jacobi--Trudi type determinant
\cite{Matsumura2018}.  The same paper introduces flagged skew Grothendieck
polynomials and shows that the tableau and determinant definitions coincide.
\name{Damir Yeliussizov} proves skew Cauchy identities for symmetric
Grothendieck polynomials and studies the associated dual filtered Young graphs
\cite{Yeliussizov2019SkewGrothendieck}.


\name{Michael Wheeler} and \name{Paul Zinn-Justin} compute
Littlewood--Richardson coefficients for double Grothendieck polynomials of
Grassmannian permutations using an integrable vertex model
\cite{WheelerZinnJustin2017}.  The Yang--Baxter equation gives product rules
for these polynomials and their duals, and the resulting coefficients are
expressed by puzzles without gashes.

\begin{problem}[See \cite{MonicalPechenikScrimshaw2018}]
Describe a \hyperref[crystals]{crystal structure}, whose connected components give stable Grothendieck polynomials.
This would be analogous to the type $A$ crystals that produce Schur polynomials.
\end{problem}

\name{Eric Marberg}, \name{Kam Hung Tong}, and \name{Tianyi Yu} introduce
normal square root crystals as a $K$-theoretic analogue of normal crystals
\cite{MarbergTongYu2025x}.  Their main theorem shows that the character of
such a crystal is a sum of symmetric Grothendieck polynomials, giving a
crystal-theoretic route to Grothendieck positivity.



\section[grothendieckDouble]{Double Grothendieck polynomials}

\begin{polydata}{grothendieckDouble}

  Symbol   & $\grothendieck_{\omega}(x|b)$ \\

The \defin{double Grothendieck polynomials} are defined via divided difference operators, $\pi_i$,
defined as
\[
\pi_i \coloneqq \frac{ (1+\beta x_{i+1})f-(1+\beta x_i)s_i(f)}{x_i - x_{i+1}},
\]
where $s_i$ permutes $x_i$ and $x_{i+1}$. We then have the base case
\[
\grothendieck_{\omega_0}(x|b) = \prod_{i+j \leq n}(x_i + b_j + \beta x_i b_j),
\]
and whenever $\length(\omega s_i) = 1 + \length(\omega)$ the recursive definition
\[
\grothendieck_{\omega}(x|b) \coloneqq \pi_i \left( \grothendieck_{\omega s_i}(x|b) \right).
\]

\name{Rui Xiong} proves Cauchy formulas for generalized double
$\beta$-Grothendieck polynomials in all types \cite{Xiong2021Grothendieck}.
These formulas imply a $K$-theoretic comodule structure map for flag
varieties and lead to a generalized Billey localization formula.
\name{Graham Hawkes} proves a generalized Cauchy identity for double
Grothendieck polynomials and gives tableau models for their stable double
limits \cite{Hawkes2024}.  The paper also introduces a half weak stable double
Grothendieck polynomial interpolating between stable and weak stable versions.
\name{Hugh Dennin} proves Cauchy identities for double Grothendieck
polynomials using pipe-dream rectification
\cite{Dennin2025Grothendieckx}.  The rectification algorithm gives a
weight-preserving bijection between the pipe-dream families suggested by the
identity, and also produces an insertion algorithm recovering a dual
RSK-type correspondence.
\name{Andrew Hardt} and \name{David Wallach} prove stability results for
products of double Grothendieck polynomials \cite{HardtWallach2025x}.  The
back- and forward-stability numbers agree with the corresponding Schubert
product stability numbers, and their work gives another proof of a finiteness
conjecture for products of back stable Grothendieck polynomials.
\name{Allen Knutson}, \name{Ezra Miller}, and \name{Alexander Yong} obtain a
formula for double Grothendieck polynomials of vexillary permutations using
flagged set-valued tableaux \cite{KnutsonMillerYong2009}.  The proof gives
a geometric interpretation through Gröbner degenerations of vexillary matrix
Schubert varieties and geometric vertex decompositions.
\name{Tomoo Matsumura} gives a tableau formula for double Grothendieck
polynomials attached to $321$-avoiding permutations \cite{Matsumura2020}.
The proof checks compatibility with the $K$-theoretic divided difference
operators.
\name{Damir Yeliussizov} proves skew Cauchy identities for symmetric
Grothendieck polynomials and studies the associated dual filtered Young
graphs \cite{Yeliussizov2019SkewGrothendieck}.

\begin{polydata}{grothendieckCanonical}

  Basis    & True \\

  Year     & 2017 \\
  Symbol   & $\grothendieckStable^{(a,b)}_\lambda(\xvec)$ \\

In \cite{Yeliussizov2017}, the author introduce a two-parameter deformation of the stable Grothendieck polynomials,
$\grothendieckStable^{(a,b)}_\lambda(\xvec)$ and the dual stable Grothendieck polynomials,
$\grothendieckDual^{(a,b)}_\lambda(\xvec)$ with the following properties.

\begin{enumerate}

\item Specializes to Schur polynomials,
$\schurS_\lambda(\xvec) = \grothendieckStable^{(0,0)}_\lambda(\xvec)$,

\item Specializes to classical Grothendieck polynomials,
$\grothendieckStable(\xvec) = \grothendieckStable^{(0,-1)}_\lambda(\xvec)$ and 
$\grothendieckDual(\xvec) = \grothendieckDual^{(0,1)}_\lambda(\xvec)$,

\item Fulfill
$\omega(\grothendieckStable(\xvec)) = \grothendieckStable^{(-1,0)}_{\lambda'}(\xvec)$ and 
$\omega(\grothendieckDual(\xvec)) = \grothendieckDual^{(1,0)}_{\lambda'}(\xvec)$,

\item Highest (lowest) degree term is a Schur polynomial,
\[
\grothendieckStable^{(a,b)}_\lambda(\xvec) = \schurS_\lambda(\xvec) + \text{higher terms} \quad
\grothendieckDual^{(a,b)}_\lambda(\xvec) = \schurS_\lambda(\xvec) + \text{lower terms}
\]

\item They are dual with respect to the \hyperref[hallInnerProduct]{Hall inner product},
$
\langle \grothendieckStable^{(-a,-b)}_\lambda, \grothendieckDual^{(a,b)}_\mu \rangle = \delta_{\lambda\mu}
$

\item Nice involution properties,
\[
\omega( \grothendieckStable^{(a,b)}_\lambda ) = \grothendieckStable^{(b,a)}_{\lambda'} \text{ and }
\omega( \grothendieckDual^{(a,b)}_\lambda ) = \grothendieckDual^{(b,a)}_{\lambda'}
\]

\item 
They have the same structure constants $c^{\nu}_{\lambda\mu}$ as the usual stable Grothendieck polynomials,
\[
\grothendieckStable^{(a,b)}_\lambda 
\grothendieckStable^{(a,b)}_\mu 
=
\sum_{\nu} (a+b)^{|\nu|-|\lambda|-|\mu|} c^{\nu}_{\lambda\mu}\grothendieckStable^{(a,b)}_\nu. 
\]
\end{enumerate}

There is a skew version of these, a Weyl-type alternant formula, and many other nice properties, such as tableau descriptions,
operator descriptions etc.

The $\grothendieckDual^{(a,b)}_\mu $ are Schur-positive, and a \hyperref[crystals]{crystal} structure on 
the canonical Stable Grothendieck polynomials, as well as their 
duals is given by \name{Graham Hawkes} and \name{Travis Scrimshaw} \cite{HawkesScrimshaw2020}.

\name{Jianping Pan}, \name{Joseph Pappe}, \name{Wencin Poh}, and
\name{Anne Schilling} introduce an uncrowding algorithm for hook-valued
tableaux \cite{PanPappePohSchilling2022}.  This gives a useful bridge between
hook-valued tableaux and ordinary semistandard-tableau data in the
Grothendieck setting.

\name{Jihyeug Jang}, \name[J. S. Kim]{Jang Soo Kim}, \name{Jianping Pan},
\name{Joseph Pappe}, and \name{Anne Schilling} relate the hook-valued-tableau
model for refined canonical stable Grothendieck polynomials to the
Schur-expansion model using uncrowding, Goulden--Greene jeu de taquin, and
tableau switching \cite{JangKimPanPappeSchilling2026}.  Their bijection
identifies the hook-valued-tableau model with pairs of a semistandard tableau
and an exquisite tableau, and yields a third model in terms of biflagged
tableaux.

\name{Byung-Hak Hwang}, \name{Jihyeug Jang}, \name[J. S. Kim]{Jang Soo Kim},
\name{Minho Song}, and \name{U-Keun Song} give further combinatorial models
for refined canonical stable Grothendieck polynomials and their duals
\cite{HwangJangKimSongSong2024x}.  Their formulas use generalized
set-valued tableaux and reverse plane partitions, and include flagged and skew
versions.

\name{Shinsuke Iwao}, \name{Kohei Motegi}, and \name{Travis Scrimshaw}
give a free-fermion presentation of refined canonical Grothendieck polynomials
and their duals, including skew versions \cite{IwaoMotegiScrimshaw2024}.
They derive skew Cauchy identities, branching rules, expansion formulas, and
integral formulas.



In \cite{GunnaZinnJustin2020}, solvable vertex models are used to 
prove Cauchy identities for the canonical Grothendieck polynomials:
\[
  \sum_{\lambda}  \grothendieckStable^{(-a,-b)}_\lambda(x_1,\dotsc,x_m) \grothendieckDual^{(a,b)}_\lambda(y_1,\dotsc,y_n) 
  = \prod_{\substack{ 1 \leq i \leq m \\ 1 \leq j \leq n }} \frac{1}{1 - x_i y_j}
\]

\[
  \sum_{\lambda}  \grothendieckStable^{(-a,-b)}_{\lambda'}(x_1,\dotsc,x_m) \grothendieckDual^{(a,b)}_\lambda(y_1,\dotsc,y_n) 
  = \prod_{\substack{ 1 \leq i \leq m \\ 1 \leq j \leq n }} (1 + x_i y_j ).
\]

\name{Siddheswar Kundu} gives a new proof of the Murnaghan--Nakayama type
rule for stable Grothendieck polynomials and proves a Murnaghan--Nakayama
type rule for canonical stable Grothendieck functions \cite{Kundu2025x}.

In \cite{LenartMaeno2006}, the authors introduce the \defin{quantum double Grothendieck polynomials}.

\name{Satoshi Naito} and \name{Daisuke Sagaki} prove a Pieri-type

\section[grothendieckFactorial]{Flagged factorial Grothendieck polynomials}


\begin{polydata}{grothendieckFactorial}
  Name     & Flagged factorial Grothendieck polynomials \\
  Space    & All \\
  Basis    & False \\
  Rating   & 1 \\
  Bib      & MatsumuraSugimoto2019 \\
  Year     & 2019 \\
  Symbol   & $\grothendieckStable_{\lambda,f}(\xvec|b)$ \\
  Category & Schubert \\
  Generalizes & grothendieckGrassmann | MatsumuraSugimoto2019 \\
  SpecializesTo & grothendieckDouble | MatsumuraSugimoto2019 | map=forget flags \\
  SpecializesTo & schurFactorial | MatsumuraSugimoto2019 | map=beta=0 \\
\end{polydata}


In \cite{MatsumuraSugimoto2019}, the authors consider a 
generalization of the \hyperref[grothendieckGrassmann]{Grassmann Grothendieck polynomials}.
They introduce the \defin{flagged factorial Grothendieck polynomials},
indexed by a partition $\lambda$ with $r$ parts and a flag $f = (0 \lt f_1 \leq f_2 \leq \dotsb \leq f_r)$.
We then let
\[
\grothendieckStable_{\lambda,f}(\xvec|b) \coloneqq \sum_{T \in SVT(\lambda,f)} \beta^{|T|-|\lambda|}[x|b]^T
\]
where the sum runs over all flagged set-valued tableaux of shape $\lambda$
and flag $f$. This means that the sets in row $i$ may only contain elements from $\{f_1,\dotsc,f_i\}$.
Furthermore,
\[
[x|b]^T \coloneqq \prod_{e\in T} x_{val(e)} \oplus b_{val(e)-r(e)+c(e)}.
\]
Here, $a\oplus b \coloneqq a+b+\beta ab$.

In \cite{MatsumuraSugimoto2019}, the authors give a Jacobi--Trudi type identity
for flagged factorial Grothendieck polynomials, and 
show that these are equal to \hyperref[grothendieckDouble]{double Grothendieck polynomials}
for vexillary ($2143$-avoiding) permutations.
\name{Peter L. Guo} and \name{Sophie C. C. Sun} prove Gustafson--Milne and
Fehér--Némethi--Rimányi type identities for factorial Grothendieck
polynomials \cite{GuoSun2018x}.  Specializing to Schur functions gives a
combinatorial proof of the Fehér--Némethi--Rimányi identity.


\section[grothendieckDualStable]{Dual stable Grothendieck polynomials}


\begin{polydata}{grothendieckDualStable}
  Name   & Dual stable Grothendieck polynomials \\
  Space    & Sym \\
  Basis    & True \\
  Rating   & 2 \\
  Bib      & LamPylyavskyy2007combinatorialhopf \\
  Year     & 2007 \\
  Symbol   & $\grothendieckDual_\lambda(\xvec)$ \\
  Category & Schur \\
  RefinedBy & grothendieckRefinedDualStable | GalashinGrinbergLiu2016 | map=t_i=1 \\
  PositiveIn & schurS | LamPylyavskyy2007combinatorialhopf \\
  Keywords & schur-positive
\end{polydata}


The dual stable Grothendieck polynomials were first studied in \cite{LamPylyavskyy2007combinatorialhopf}.
They are defined through the \hyperref[hallInnerProduct]{Hall inner product}
\[
\langle \grothendieckDual_\lambda, \grothendieckStable_\mu \rangle = \delta_{\lambda \mu}.
\]

Let $\mathrm{RPP}(\lambda)$ be the set of fillings of $\lambda$ with weakly increasing rows and columns.
Then
\[
\grothendieckDual_\lambda(\xvec) \coloneqq \sum_{T \in \mathrm{RPP}(\lambda)} \xvec^{ev(T)}
\]
where $ev(T)_i$is the number of columns of $T$ where $i$ appears.
Note that
\[
\grothendieckDual_\lambda(\xvec) = \schurS_\lambda(\xvec) + \text{lower order terms}.
\]
The skew version $\grothendieckDual_{\lambda/\mu}(\xvec)$ is defined in a similar manner.
One can prove that $\grothendieckDual_{\lambda/\mu}(\xvec)$ is symmetric by using a  version
of the Bender--Knuth involutions, see \cite{GalashinGrinbergLiu2016}.

\name{Jason Bandlow} and \name{Jennifer Morse} expand modified
Hall--Littlewood functions in the dual stable Grothendieck basis
\cite{BandlowMorse2010}.  They extend the charge statistic to set-valued
tableaux, giving a signed combinatorial expansion in this basis.


In \cite{LiMorseShields2016}, it is shown that the constants appearing in the expansion
\[
\grothendieckDual_{\nu/\mu}(\xvec) = \sum_{\nu} 
(-1)^{|\lambda|+|\mu|-|\nu|}  c^{\nu}_{\mu\lambda} \grothendieckDual_{\lambda}(\xvec),
\]
agree with the coefficients $c_{\lambda\mu}^{\nu}$ in 
the \hyperref[grothendieckStableLittlewoodRichardson]{Littlewood--Richardson rule for stable Grothendieck polynomials}.
\name{Motoki Takigiku} explains Pieri coefficients for stable and dual
stable Grothendieck polynomials \cite{Takigiku2018x}.  In this description,
the lower-degree terms are obtained from the leading terms by alternating
sums of meets or joins.


\subsection[grothendieckDualStableBialternant]{Bialternant formula}


A \defin{bialternant formula} for the dual stable Grothendieck polynomials 
is proved in \cite[Thm. 17]{Yeliussizov2019}.
It states that
\begin{align}
\grothendieckDual_{\lambda}(\xvec) = \frac{1}{ \prod_{i \lt j} (x_i-x_j)}
\det\left[
\phi^{i-1}x_j^{\lambda_i+n-i}
\right]_{1\leq i,j \leq n}
\end{align}
where $\phi^k x^n = \sum_{j=0}^n \binom{k+j-1}{j}x^{n-j}$.


\subsection[grothendieckDualStableLittlewood]{Littlewood-type identities}


Analogous to the \hyperref[schurSums]{Schur function Littlewood identities}, we 
have 
\[
\sum_{\lambda : \length(\lambda)\leq n} \grothendieckDual_\lambda(x_1,\dotsc,x_m) = \prod_{i=1}^m \frac{1}{(1-z_i)^n}.
\]



\subsection[grothendieckDualStableJacobiTrudi]{Jacobi--Trudi identity}

There is a Jacobi--Trudi type identity, \cite[Prop. 4.4]{Iwao2019} given by
\begin{align}
\grothendieckDual_\lambda(\xvec) = 
\det\left[
\sum_{m=0}^\infty \binom{1-i}{m} \beta^m \completeH_{\lambda_i-i+j-m}(\xvec)
\right]_{1\leq i,j \leq n}.
\end{align}

Alternatively, \cite{Yeliussizov2017,AmanovYeliussizov2020}, we have 
\begin{align}
\grothendieckDual_\lambda(\xvec) = 
\det\left[
\completeH_{\lambda_i-i+j}(1^{\lambda_i-1},\xvec)
\right]_{1\leq i,j \leq \length(\lambda)}
=
\det\left[
\elementaryE_{\lambda'_i-i+j}(1^{\lambda'_i-1},\xvec)
\right]_{1\leq i,j \leq \lambda_1}.
\end{align}

\name{Damir Yeliussizov} applies dual stable Grothendieck polynomials and
normalized Schur functions to random lozenge tilings and plane partitions
\cite{Yeliussizov2021}.  In particular, the paper determines certain corner
distributions and introduces related distributions connected with the corner
growth model.

There is also a Jacobi--Trudi identity for the \defin{skew dual stable Grothendieck polynomials},
originally conjectured by \name{Darij Grinberg} \href{http://www.cip.ifi.lmu.de/~grinberg/algebra/chicago2015.pdf}{(see these slides)}.
A proof was later presented in \name[J. S. Kim]{Jang Soo Kim}
\cite{Kim2021RefinedDualGrothendieckJT}.
A different proof was obtained independently by \name{Alimzhan Amanov} and \name{Damir Yeliussizov} \cite{AmanovYeliussizov2020}.
Both approaches use quite novel techniques with \hyperref[lgvLemma]{non-intersecting lattice paths}.



The skew Jacobi--Trudi identity states that
\begin{align}
\grothendieckDual_{\lambda/\mu}(\xvec) = 
\det\left[
\elementaryE_{\lambda'_i-i-\mu'_j+j}(1^{\lambda'_i-\mu'_j-1},\xvec)
\right]_{1\leq i,j \leq \lambda_1}.
\end{align}


The skew Jacobi--Trudi identity extends to the family 
of \defin{refined dual stable Grothendieck polynomials},
introduced in \cite{GalashinGrinbergLiu2016}, see
\cite{Kim2021RefinedDualGrothendieckJT,AmanovYeliussizov2020} for proofs.
\name[J. S. Kim]{Jang Soo Kim} later proves Jacobi--Trudi formulas for
flagged refined dual stable Grothendieck polynomials
\cite{Kim2022FlaggedRefinedDualGrothendieckJT}, using plethystic substitution
and resolving a conjecture of \name{Darij Grinberg}.


\subsection[dualGrothendieckSchurExpansion]{Schur expansion}

In \cite[Thm. 9.8]{LamPylyavskyy2007combinatorialhopf}, the authors show that 
\[
\grothendieckDual_\lambda(\xvec) = \sum_\mu f^\mu_\lambda \schurS_\mu(\xvec)
\]
where $f^\mu_\lambda $ is the number of \defin{elegant fillings} of shape $\lambda/\mu$.
A filling is elegant if it is semi-standard, and all entries in row $i$ are in $1,\dotsc,i-1$.
See also \cite{Galashin2017} for a crystal proof.


A flagged version of the skew dual Grothendieck polynomials are 
shown to be \hyperref[key]{key positive} in \cite{Kundu2023x}.


\subsection[dualGrothendieckPercolation]{Last passage percolation}

\name{Damir Yeliussizov} \cite{Yeliussizov2020} show that the dual Grothendieck polynomials
can be realized as a column distribution of a directed last-passage percolation model.


\section[grothendieckRefinedDualStable]{Generalized and refined dual stable Grothendieck polynomials}


\begin{polydata}{grothendieckRefinedDualStable}
  Name   & Generalized and refined dual stable Grothendieck polynomials \\
  Space    & Sym \\
  Basis    & True \\
  Rating   & 1 \\
  Bib      & GalashinGrinbergLiu2016 \\
  Year     & 2016 \\
  Symbol   & $\grothendieckRefinedDualStable_\lambda(\xvec)$ \\
  Category & Schur \\
  Keywords & schur-positive
\end{polydata}


The \defin{refined dual stable Grothendieck polynomials} were introduced in \cite{GalashinGrinbergLiu2016}.
They define these as 
\[
\grothendieckRefinedDualStable_\lambda(\xvec;\tvec) \coloneqq 
\sum_{T \in RPP(\lambda)}  \xvec^{ev(T)} \tvec^{b(T)}
\]
where (as usual), $ev(T)_i$ is the number of columns containing $i$,
and $b(T)_j$ is the number of boxes in row $j$ whose entry is equal to the entry in 
box immediately below it. 


In \cite{Yeliussizov2019}, the \defin{generalized dual stable Grothendieck polynomials}
are defined:
\[
\grothendieckDual_\lambda(\xvec;\zvec)
\coloneqq \sum_{\pi \in PP(\lambda)} \prod_{(i,j) \in \DES(\pi)} x_i z_{\pi(i,j)}.
\]
Note that $\grothendieckDual_\lambda(1^n;\zvec) =  \grothendieckDual_\lambda(\zvec)$.
It is observed that $\grothendieckDual_\lambda(\xvec;\zvec) = \xvec^{\lambda}\grothendieckRefinedDualStable_\lambda(\xvec^{-1},\zvec)$,
where $\grothendieckRefinedDualStable_\lambda$ is a refined dual stable Grothendieck polynomial.


Yeliussizov (\cite[Eq. (11)]{Yeliussizov2019}) prove that
\[
\sum_{\lambda : \length(\lambda)\leq n} \grothendieckDual_\lambda(x_1,\dotsc,x_m; z_1,\dotsc,z_n) = 
\prod_{i=1}^m \prod_{j=1}^n  \frac{1}{1-x_iz_j}.
\]
Yeliussizov proves several new $q$-identities involving
plane partitions and dual Grothendieck polynomials in \cite{Yeliussizov2019}.
\name{Alimzhan Amanov} and \name{Damir Yeliussizov} introduce
higher-dimensional analogues of dual Grothendieck polynomials
\cite{AmanovYeliussizov2023}.  These are quasisymmetric functions whose
specializations enumerate higher-dimensional partitions of fixed shape.



\name{Kohei Motegi} and \name{Travis Scrimshaw} introduce an integrable
vertex model whose partition function is the refined dual stable
Grothendieck polynomial \cite{MotegiScrimshaw2025}.  They prove Cauchy and
Jacobi--Trudi identities for skew refined dual stable Grothendieck
polynomials, and relate the same model to a Schur measure.
