The \defin{key polynomials}\label{keyPolynomial}, also known as
\defin{Demazure characters}\label{demazureCharacter}, form a family of
nonsymmetric polynomials, indexed by
\hyperref[weakCompositions]{weak compositions}, or alternatively, by an
integer partition and a permutation.  The set
$\{ \key_\alpha(x_1,\dotsc,x_n) \}_\alpha$, where $\alpha$ ranges over all
\hyperref[weakCompositions]{weak compositions} of length $n$, is a basis for
$\setC[x_1,\dotsc,x_n]$.

The key polynomials generalize the \hyperref[schurS]{Schur polynomials}.

Demazure characters were introduced in \cite{Demazure1974nouvelle,Demazure1974}.
Geometrically, they are characters of Demazure modules associated with
Schubert varieties in the \hyperref[flagVarieties]{flag variety}; see
\hyperref[varietiesForKeys]{varieties in algebraic combinatorics}.
Later, a combinatorial formula using so-called
\defin{key tableaux}\label{keyTableau} was discovered in
\cite{Lascoux1990Keys}.
Key polynomials are also given as a specialization of
\hyperref[macdonaldE]{nonsymmetric Macdonald polynomials};
see \cite{HaglundHaimanLoehr2008}.  This gives several combinatorial
models for the key polynomials; see the work of \name{Sarah Mason}
\cite{Mason2009}, and also \cite{Kurland2016,AlexanderssonSawhney2019}.
Another positive model is given by \hyperref[kohnertTableaux]{Kohnert tableaux},
which label the Kohnert diagrams for the key diagram.
The same Kohnert-tableau framework gives positive expansions into the
\hyperref[quasiKey]{quasi-key basis} and into the
\hyperref[slideF]{fundamental slide basis} \cite{AssafSearles2018}.

\name[Mrigendra Singh Kushwaha]{Mrigendra Singh Kushwaha},
\name[K. N. Raghavan]{K.~N.~Raghavan}, and \name{Sankaran Viswanath} give
simple procedures for computing the left and right keys of a semistandard Young
tableau \cite{KushwahaRaghavanViswanath2023x}.  Their procedures repeatedly
apply Deodhar lifts, which recover the final and initial directions of the
corresponding Littelmann paths.

Key polynomials are closely related to \hyperref[atom]{Demazure atoms}.

The $K$-theoretic versions of key polynomials are the
\hyperref[lascoux]{Lascoux polynomials}.

\name{Milo Bechtloff Weising} introduces the almost symmetric Schur
functions $s_{(\mu|\lambda)}$ \cite{Weising2024AlmostSymmetricx}.  They are the $q=t=0$
specialization of stable-limit nonsymmetric Macdonald functions and
simultaneously generalize finite-variable key polynomials and ordinary
Schur functions.  The paper gives recurrence definitions using isobaric
divided differences and Weyl symmetrization operators, a tableau formula,
and positive expansions in the monomial and monomial-Schur bases for almost
symmetric functions.



\subsection[keyDefinitionOperators]{Definition (Operators)}

Define the \hyperref[dividedDifferenceOperators]{divided difference operator}
$\partial_i$ as
\[
 \partial_i(f) = \frac{f - s_i(f)}{z_i - z_{i+1}},
\]
and let the \defin{isobaric divided difference operator}
\label{isobaricDividedDifferenceOperator} be
$\pi_i(f) \coloneqq \partial_i(z_i f)$ for $i=1,\dotsc,n-1$ whenever
$f \in \setC[z_1,\dotsc,z_n]$.
The operators satisfy $\pi_i^2 = \pi_i$ and the
\hyperref[braidRelations]{braid relations}.

Let $\sigma = s_{i_1} s_{i_2} \dotsc s_{i_\ell}$ be a \hyperref[reducedWord]{reduced word} of a permutation $\sigma \in \symS_n$,
and let
\[
 \pi_\sigma \coloneqq \pi_{i_1} \circ \pi_{i_2} \circ \dotsb \circ \pi_{i_\ell}.
\]
This is well-defined, as the action of $\pi_\sigma$ is independent of the choice of reduced word.


Let $\lambda$ be a partition with at most $n$ parts, and let $\sigma \in \symS_n$.
In fact, we can choose $\sigma$ to be the \emph{minimal coset representation} for
the parabolic subgroup $W_J \subseteq \symS_n$ that fixes $\lambda$.
The \defin{key polynomial}\label{keyPolynomialOperatorDefinition}
$\key_{\lambda,\sigma}(z)$ is defined as
\begin{equation*}
 \key_{\lambda,\sigma}(z) \coloneqq \pi_\sigma\left( z_1^{\lambda_1} \dotsm z_n^{\lambda_n} \right).
\end{equation*}
Note that if $\sigma^1(\lambda)=\sigma^2(\lambda)$, then
$\key_{\lambda,\sigma^1}(z) = \key_{\lambda,\sigma^2}(z)$.


This definition shows the close connection between \hyperref[schubert]{Schubert polynomials}
and key polynomials.
If $w_0$ is the longest permutation in $\symS_n$, then
\[
 \schurS_\lambda(z_1,\dotsc,z_n) = \key_{\lambda,w_0}(z_1,\dotsc,z_n),
\]
so key polynomials generalize the \hyperref[schurS]{Schur polynomials}.



\begin{example}[From \cite{AlexanderssonAlhajjar2018}]

Let $\lambda = (2,1,0,0)$ and $\sigma = [2,4,3,1] \in \symS_4$ in one-line notation.
The permutation can be expressed as a reduced word as
$\sigma = s_2 s_3 s_2 s_1$.
We compute the key polynomial as follows:
\begin{align*}
 \key_{\lambda,\sigma}(z) &= \pi_2 \pi_3 \pi_2 \pi_1 (z_1^2 z_2) =
 \pi_2 \pi_3 \pi_2 \partial_1 (z_1^3 z_2 ) = \pi_2 \pi_3 \pi_2 \left( \frac{z_1^3z_2 - z_1 z^3_2}{z_1-z_2}\right) \\
 &= \pi_2 \pi_3 \pi_2  (z_1^2z_2 + z_1 z_2^2).
\end{align*}
We continue the calculation by applying $\pi_2$ and get
\begin{align*}
\key_{\lambda,\sigma}(z) &=
 \pi_2 \pi_3 (z_2 z_1^2+z_3 z_1^2+z_2^2 z_1+z_3^2 z_1+z_2 z_3 z_1).
 \end{align*}
 Applying $\pi_2 \pi_3$ then finally gives
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
\key_{\lambda,\sigma}(z) &=
z_1^2 z_2  +
z_1^2 z_3  +
z_1^2 z_4  +
z_1 z_2^2  +
z_1 z_3^2  \\
&\phantom{=}+
z_1 z_4^2  +
z_1 z_2 z_3  +
z_1 z_2 z_4  +
z_1 z_3 z_4 .
 \end{split}
\end{equation*}
In general, some monomials may appear multiple times.
\end{example}


Alternatively, key polynomials are also commonly indexed by \hyperref[weakCompositions]{weak compositions}.
If $\alpha = (\alpha_1,\dotsc,\alpha_n)$ is a weak composition,
we let $\key_{\alpha}(z)$ be the key polynomial $\key_{\lambda,\sigma}(z)$,
where $\lambda$ is the partition obtained from $\alpha$ by sorting the elements in decreasing order,
and $\sigma \in \symS_n$ is a permutation such that it sorts $\alpha$
into a partition; $\lambda = (\alpha_{\sigma(1)},\alpha_{\sigma(2)},\dotsc,\alpha_{\sigma(n)})$.

For example, $\alpha = (1,0,3,2)$ corresponds to $\lambda = (3,2,1,0)$ and $\sigma = [3, 4, 1, 2]$.
With these conventions, $\key_{\lambda}(z)=\schurS_{\lambda}(z)$.

\emph{Note:} This indexing convention is not uniform in the literature;
in some places, one needs to reverse the indexing composition $\alpha$.




\subsection[keyDefinitionGT]{Kogan faces and key polynomials via GT-polytopes}

A formula for the key polynomials
as a sum over lattice points in a union of faces
of \hyperref[gtpatterns]{Gelfand--Tsetlin polytopes} was proved in \cite{KiritchenkoSmirnovTimorin2010}.
This subsection describes the construction in detail,
following \cite{KiritchenkoSmirnovTimorin2010} and \cite{Kogan2000schubertgeometry}.

Recall that the \hyperref[gtpolytopes]{GT-polytope} $\GT(\lambda)$
is defined by the interlacing inequalities
$x_{i+1,j} \geq x_{i,j} \geq x_{i+1,j+1}$
together with the boundary condition $x_{n,j} = \lambda_j$.
Its integer points are in bijection with \hyperref[prelimTableaux]{semistandard Young tableaux} of shape $\lambda$.
The \hyperref[gtpatterns]{weight} of a GT-pattern $G$ is
$w(G) = (w_1, \dotsc, w_n)$ where
$w_k = \sum_j x_{k,j} - \sum_j x_{k-1,j}$
(row sum differences, with $\sum_j x_{0,j} = 0$).

\begin{definition}[Kogan face]\label{def:koganFace}\label{koganFace}
A \defin{Kogan face} of $\GT(\lambda)$ is obtained by forcing
a subset of the \emph{left-interlacing} inequalities to equalities:
$x_{i+1,j} = x_{i,j}$
for some collection of pairs $(i,j)$ with $1 \leq j \leq i \lt n$.
To each equality $x_{i+1,j} = x_{i,j}$, we associate the simple transposition
$s_{n-i+j-1}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{example*}[Equalities and transpositions in a GT-pattern]
For $n = 4$, the six left-interlacing positions and their transpositions
($s_{4-i+j-1} = s_{3-i+j}$) are:
\begin{figure}
\svgimg[width=0.2\textwidth]{svg-images/koganFacesGT-1.svg}{Left-interlacing inequalities in a GT-pattern for $n=4$,
with transposition labels.}
\end{figure}
The blue arrows show the left-interlacing inequalities $x_{i+1,j} \geq x_{i,j}$,
labeled with the transposition $s_{n-i+j-1}$ that is activated when the
inequality is forced to equality.
\end{example*}


\begin{definition}[Reduced Kogan face and type]\label{def:reducedKogan}
\label{reducedKoganFace}
Given a Kogan face, we read the associated transpositions row by row from
bottom to top.
Within each row, we read from left to right.
If the resulting word $s_{a_1} s_{a_2} \dotsm s_{a_\ell}$
is a \hyperref[reducedWord]{reduced word}, the Kogan face is called
\defin{reduced}.
Its \defin{type}\label{koganType} is the permutation
$\sigma = s_{a_1} s_{a_2} \dotsm s_{a_\ell} \in \symS_n$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
The same permutation may arise as the type of several
different reduced Kogan faces, since a permutation may have multiple reduced words.
\end{remark}

\subsubsection[keyKogan]{Key polynomials from Kogan faces}

The key polynomial $\key_{\lambda,\sigma}$ is indexed by a partition $\lambda$
and a permutation $\sigma \in \symS_n$.
The connection to Kogan faces uses the product $w_0 \sigma$,
where we use function composition:
$(w_0 \sigma)(i) = w_0(\sigma(i))$; that is, $\sigma$ acts first.

\begin{theorem}[Kiritchenko--Smirnov--Timorin {\cite[Thm.~2.2]{KiritchenkoSmirnovTimorin2010}}]
\label{thm:keyGT}\label{keyGelfandTsetlinFormula}
Define
\[
  \GT(\lambda, \sigma) \coloneqq
  \bigcup_{\substack{\mathcal{F} \subseteq \GT(\lambda) \\
    \mathcal{F} \text{ reduced Kogan face} \\
    \type(\mathcal{F}) = w_0 \sigma}} \mathcal{F}.
\]
Then
\[
  \key_{\lambda,\sigma}(\mathbf{z}) =
  \sum_{G \in \GT(\lambda,\sigma) \cap \setZ^{n(n-1)/2}}
  z_1^{w_1(G)} \dotsm z_n^{w_n(G)}.
\]
\end{theorem}


\begin{example*}[Continuing the previous example]
Continuing with $n = 3$, $\lambda = 210$:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\sigma = \id = [1,2,3]$:
  $w_0 \sigma = w_0 = [3,2,1]$.
  Unique face of type $w_0$: $\{L_1, L_2, L_3\}$.
  One pattern: $(a,b,c) = (2,1,2)$ giving $z_1^2 z_2$.
  So $\key_{\lambda, \id} = z_1^2 z_2$.

\item $\sigma = s_1 = [2,1,3]$:
  $w_0 \sigma = [2,3,1] = s_1 s_2$.
  Unique face of type $s_1 s_2$: $\{L_1, L_2\}$.
  Patterns: $a=2, b=1$, $c \in \{1,2\}$
  giving $z_1 z_2^2 + z_1^2 z_2$.
  So $\key_{\lambda, s_1} = z_1^2 z_2 + z_1 z_2^2$.

\item $\sigma = s_2 = [1,3,2]$:
  $w_0 \sigma = [3,1,2] = s_2 s_1$.
  Unique face of type $s_2 s_1$: $\{L_1, L_3\}$.
  Patterns: $a=2, c=2$, $b \in \{0,1\}$
  giving $z_1^2 z_3 + z_1^2 z_2$.
  So $\key_{\lambda, s_2} = z_1^2 z_2 + z_1^2 z_3$.

\item $\sigma = w_0 = [3,2,1]$:
  $w_0 \sigma = \id$.
  The full GT-polytope (type $\id$, no equalities).
  All 8 patterns.
  $\key_{\lambda, w_0} = \schurS_{(2,1)}(z_1, z_2, z_3)$.
\end{itemize}
All four results match the operator definition
$\key_{\lambda,\sigma} = \pi_\sigma(z_1^{\lambda_1} \dotsm z_n^{\lambda_n})$.
\end{example*}

\begin{remark}[Convention note]
We use \emph{function composition}: the product $\pi\sigma$ means
$(\pi\sigma)(i) = \pi(\sigma(i))$, so $\sigma$ acts first.
This is the same convention as matrix multiplication.
Some references use the opposite convention where $\pi$ acts first in $\pi\sigma$;
this interchanges key polynomials for $\sigma$ and $\sigma^{-1}$
(or equivalently, left and right keys in the tableaux model).
Our convention matches the standard Demazure operator definition
$\key_{\lambda,\sigma} = \pi_\sigma(z^\lambda)$
when $\pi_\sigma = \pi_{i_1} \circ \dotsb \circ \pi_{i_\ell}$
for a reduced word $\sigma = s_{i_1} \dotsm s_{i_\ell}$.
\end{remark}

\name{Valentina Kiritchenko} constructs simple geometric mitosis operations on
faces of Cayley sums of polytopes \cite{Kiritchenko2025}.  These operations
act as convex-geometric counterparts of divided difference operators and give
a uniform construction of Knutson--Miller mitosis in type $A$ and Fujita
mitosis in type $C$ on Kogan faces.


\subsubsection[keyKempf]{Kempf permutations and flagged Schur polynomials}

\begin{proposition}[Kogan {\cite{Kogan2000schubertgeometry}}]\label{prop:kempfCase}
If $\sigma \in \symS_n$ avoids the \hyperref[permutationPattern]{permutation pattern} $132$,
then there is a \emph{unique} reduced Kogan face of type $w_0 \sigma$.
In this case $\GT(\lambda, \sigma)$ is a single polytope face,
and the key polynomial is a \hyperref[schurFlagged]{flagged Schur polynomial}.
\end{proposition}


\subsubsection[keyEhrhart]{Ehrhart polynomial}

Fix $\lambda$, $\sigma \in \symS_n$.
With the above theorem, it follows that the map $k \mapsto \key_{k \lambda,\sigma}(1^n)$
is a polynomial in $k$.
This is in fact an \hyperref[ehrhart]{Ehrhart polynomial} of a union of faces
in a GT-polytope.
Let $P_{\sigma}(\lambda_1,\dotsc,\lambda_n;k)$ denote this polynomial.
From the definition via divided difference operators,
one can see that $P_{\sigma}(\lambda_1,\dotsc,\lambda_n;k)$
is a polynomial in $\setQ[\lambda_1,\dotsc,\lambda_n,k]$.

See \cite{AlexanderssonAlhajjar2018} for the following conjecture.
\begin{conjecture}[Alexandersson--Alhajjar (2018)]
The polynomial $P_{\sigma}(\lambda_1,\dotsc,\lambda_n;k)$ has nonnegative coefficients.

Moreover, after setting $a_j = \lambda_1+\lambda_2+\dotsb+\lambda_j$, the function
$P_{\sigma}(a_1,\dotsc,a_n;k)$ is a polynomial in $\setQ[a_1,\dotsc,a_n,k]$
with nonnegative coefficients.
\end{conjecture}
See also the \hyperref[keyRealRootedHstar]{related conjecture}
regarding the corresponding $h^*$-polynomial.


\subsection[keyDefinitionKeyFillings]{Definition (Skyline fillings)}

In \cite{HaglundHaimanLoehr2008}, the authors presented a combinatorial model for the
\hyperref[macdonaldE]{nonsymmetric Macdonald polynomials}.
Setting $q=t=0$ in the nonsymmetric Macdonald polynomial
$\macdonaldE_{\alpha}(\xvec,q,t)$ recovers the key polynomial
$\key_\alpha(\xvec)$.


From the work of \name{Sarah Mason} \cite{Mason2009},
we in fact get two combinatorial models for key polynomials
as special cases of the \hyperref[macdonaldEperm]{permuted basement Macdonald polynomials}.
In the definition below, we assume that the reader is familiar with
the combinatorial model for the permuted basement Macdonald polynomials.


\begin{definition}
A \defin{semistandard augmented filling}\label{semistandardAugmentedFilling}
of shape $\alpha = (\alpha_1,\dotsc,\alpha_n)$
is a non-attacking filling of the diagram with shape $\alpha$,
basement $\sigma = [n,n-1,\dotsc,2,1]$ such that all rows (including the basement)
are weakly decreasing, and there are no coinversion triples.
Let $\mathrm{SSAF}(\alpha)$ be the set of semistandard augmented fillings
of shape $\alpha$.

The key polynomial $\key_\alpha(\xvec)$ is defined as
\[
\key_\alpha(\xvec) = \sum_{T \in \mathrm{SSAF}(\alpha)} \xvec^T.
\]
When $\alpha$ is a partition, we sum over fillings with
weakly decreasing rows, and strictly decreasing columns.
This sum is the Schur polynomial $\schurS_{\lambda}(x_1,\dotsc,x_n)$.

Alternatively, by choosing a different basement,
we can realize $\key_\alpha(\xvec)$ as a sum over augmented fillings
with partition shape.
Let $\mathrm{SSAF}_{\sigma}(\lambda)$ be the set of semistandard augmented fillings
with basement $\sigma$ and shape $\lambda$.
Then
\[
\key_\alpha(\xvec) = \sum_{T \in \mathrm{SSAF}_{\sigma}(\lambda)} \xvec^T,
\quad
\text{where} \quad
\alpha = (\lambda_{\sigma^{-1}(n)}, \lambda_{\sigma^{-1}(n-1)},\dotsc,\lambda_{\sigma^{-1}(1)}).
\]
\end{definition}

A series of bijections proves the equivalence of the two above formulas;
see \cite[Prop.~5.5]{AlexanderssonSawhney2019} (with $t=0$).
This amounts to proving that interchanging two adjacent rows
(including the basement) in an augmented diagram in such a way that a
longer row is moved upwards,
preserves the weighted sum of SSAFs with corresponding shape and basement.

\begin{array}{c|ccccc}

\end{example*}

[1,3]/[3] & (1,0,2) & (1,0,2) & \text{yes} \\
[2,2]/[3] & (0,2,1) & (0,2,1) & \text{no} \\
[2,3]/[3] & (0,1,2) & (0,1,2) & \text{no}
\end{array}
\]
The five tableaux marked ``yes'' have weights
$(2,1,0)$, $(2,0,1)$, $(1,2,0)$, $(1,1,1)$, and $(1,0,2)$,
giving the same polynomial as the SSAF computation above.
\end{example*}



For classical background on tableaux, Gelfand--Tsetlin patterns, and branching
rules around key-tableau combinatorics, see \cite{Proctor1994}.  The
Bruhat-order side of the divided-difference formulas and the standard
elementary monomials below is closely related to the chain constructions of
\name{Alexander Postnikov} and \name{Richard P. Stanley}
\cite{PostnikovStanley2008}.


\subsection[keyDefinitionSSYT]{Definition (SSYT)}

A \defin{key tableau}\label{keyTableauSSYTDefinition} is a semistandard
tableau such that the entries in column $j+1$ are a subset of the entries in
column $j$, for all $j$.

Below is the unique key tableau with weight $(1,3,0,1,2,4)$:
\begin{figure}
\begin{ytableau}
1 & 2 & 2 & 6\\
2 & 5 & 6 \\
4 & 6 \\
5 \\
6 \\
\end{ytableau}
\end{figure}
The map that sends a key to its weight is a bijection between the set of key
tableaux and the set of compositions.  Given a composition
$\alpha = (\alpha_1,\dotsc,\alpha_\ell)$, place a cell $j$ in the first
$\alpha_j$ columns.
This produces a unique key tableau, $\mathrm{key}(\alpha)$, of shape $\sort(\alpha)$.

\name{Reuven Hodges} and \name{Alexander Yong} characterize
multiplicity-free key polynomials \cite{HodgesYong2022}.  They also relate
Coxeter combinatorics to spherical Schubert geometry
\cite{HodgesYong2020Spherical}.


Given a semistandard tableau $T$, we can compute its
\defin{right key}\label{rightKey} $K_+(T)$,
via a direct but involved process.  A simpler method is proved in
\cite{Mason2009}.
The key polynomial can then be computed by
\[
  \key_\alpha(\xvec) =
  \sum_{\substack{T \in \SSYT(\sort(\alpha)) \\ K_+(T) \leq \mathrm{key}(\alpha)}} \xvec^T
\]
where we sum over all \hyperref[prelimTableaux]{semistandard tableaux} $T$
whose right key, $K_+(T)$, is entrywise weakly smaller than $\mathrm{key}(\alpha)$.

Similarly, the Demazure atoms are given as the sum over
\hyperref[prelimTableaux]{semistandard tableaux} with a given right key:
\[
  \atom_\alpha(\xvec) =
  \sum_{\substack{T \in \SSYT(\sort(\alpha)) \\ K_+(T) = \mathrm{key}(\alpha)}} \xvec^T
\]



\subsection[keyFromKostant]{Keys via Kostant partition function}

Let $\Phi^+=\{ \evec_i-\evec_j : 1 \leq i < j \leq n\}$ be the positive roots of the
\hyperref[rootSystem]{root system} of type $A_{n-1}$.
For a multiset $\Gamma$ of roots in $\Phi^+$, let $\kostantP_\Gamma(\nu)$ be the
corresponding \hyperref[kostantPartitionFunction]{Kostant partition function},
defined by
\[
\prod_{\gamma \in \Gamma} \frac{1}{1-\xvec^{-\gamma}}
= \sum_{\nu} \kostantP_\Gamma(\nu)\xvec^{-\nu}.
\]
This is a vector partition function in the sense of \cite{Sturmfels1995}.

Fix a word $i_1,\dotsc,i_\ell$ such that
\[
 \key_\alpha(\xvec)=\pi_{i_\ell}\dotsm\pi_{i_1}(\xvec^\lambda),
\]
where $\lambda=\sort(\alpha)$.
Writing $\delta_i=\evec_i-\evec_{i+1}$, the operator identity
\[
\pi_i(F)= \frac{F}{1-\xvec^{-\delta_i}} - \frac{\xvec^{-\delta_i}s_i(F)}{1-\xvec^{-\delta_i}}
\]
lets us expand one Demazure operator at a time.
If a reflected denominator root becomes negative, rewrite it using
\[
\frac{1}{1-\xvec^\gamma} = -\frac{\xvec^{-\gamma}}{1-\xvec^{-\gamma}}.
\]
After all roots are made positive, this gives a finite signed list of triples
$(c,\beta,\Gamma)$ with
\[
\key_\alpha(\xvec) = \sum_{(c,\beta,\Gamma)} c\,\xvec^\beta \prod_{\gamma \in \Gamma}\frac{1}{1-\xvec^{-\gamma}}.
\]

\begin{example}[Key polynomial from root system]
The key polynomial $\key_{02}=\pi_1(x_1^2)$ gives
\[
\key_{02}
=
\frac{x_1^2}{1-x_2/x_1}
-
\frac{x_1^{-1}x_2^3}{1-x_2/x_1}
=x_1^2+x_1x_2+x_2^2.
\]
\end{example}

\subsection[keyProduct]{Products of key polynomials}

A product of key polynomials is not in general key-positive.
However, in some instances such a product is key-positive; see \cite{Kouno2018}.

\name{Guilherme Zeus Dantas e Moura}'s senior thesis surveys operator,
tableau, plactic, and crystal models for key and atom polynomials
\cite{DantasEMoura2024Thesis}.  The product-coefficient problem is a central
motivation: products of two key polynomials need not be key-positive, but the
expansion of a product of two key polynomials in the Demazure atom basis is
expected to have nonnegative coefficients.


\begin{conjecture}[Attributed to unpublished work by Reiner--Shimozono]
A product of two key polynomials expands positively into Demazure atoms.
\end{conjecture}
See \cite{Pun2016Thesis} for special cases of this result (she verifies the conjecture in $\setZ[x_1,x_2,x_3]$).

\name{Timothy C. Miller} proves a positive combinatorial rule for multiplying
a permuted-basement Demazure atom by a Schur polynomial
\cite{Miller2025x}.  The rule is given by a compatible pair of vertex models
for the atom and the Schur factor, and it specializes to product rules for
ordinary Demazure atoms and characters.
\name{Rosa Paten} and \name{Dora Woodruff} extend several Schur-positivity
results to key positivity by replacing Schur polynomials with flagged Schur
polynomials \cite{PatenWoodruff2026x}.  In particular, they prove key
positivity for many Temperley--Lieb immanants of flagged Jacobi--Trudi
matrices, give a key-expansion rule for most products of flagged skew Schur
polynomials, and prove a related log-concavity result using Demazure crystals.



\subsection[keyPieri]{Pieri rule for key polynomials}

A Pieri rule for key polynomials, described via an
\hyperref[rsk]{Robinson--Schensted--Knuth}-type algorithm, is given by
\name{Sami Assaf} and \name{Danjoseph Quijada} in \cite{AssafQuijada2019x}.
Their rule expands the product of an arbitrary key polynomial with a
single-part key polynomial in the key basis.  Unlike general key products,
this Pieri case admits a cancellation-free, multiplicity-free formula.  The
proof replaces ordinary row insertion with an insertion algorithm on
\hyperref[kohnert]{Kohnert diagrams}.

\name{Noah Cape} and \name{Shaul Zemel} study generating series of key
polynomials in a modified Demazure algebra \cite{CapeZemel2024x}.  For a
fixed permutation $w$, the generating function
$K_w(\xvec,\tvec)=\sum_\lambda K_{\lambda,w}(\xvec)\tvec^\lambda$ is a
rational function whose denominator is described by bounded ascending
sequences determined by the first values of $w$ \cite[Thm.~3.9]{CapeZemel2024x}.
The numerator has structured quadratic terms governed by multisets with
multiple presentations as sums of such bounded sequences.

\section[completeFlaggedHomogeneous]{Complete flagged homogeneous polynomials}

\begin{polydata}{completeFlaggedHomogeneous}
  Name     & Complete flagged homogeneous polynomials \\
  Space    & All \\
  Basis    & True \\
  Rating   & 1 \\

  Year     & 2023 \\
  Symbol   & $h_a(\xvec)$ \\

\name{Henry Ehrhard} \cite{Ehrhard2023x} introduces
\defin{complete flagged homogeneous polynomials}
\label{completeFlaggedHomogeneousPolynomial}.  For a weak composition
$a=(a_1,a_2,\dotsc)$, they are defined by
\[
h_a(\xvec) \coloneqq \prod_{i\geq 1} \completeH_{a_i}(x_1,\dotsc,x_i).
\]
They form a $\setZ$-basis for the polynomial ring, and their stable limit is
the usual \hyperref[completeH]{complete homogeneous symmetric function}
$\completeH_{\sort(a)}$.

Ehrhard gives a nonnegative expansion of these polynomials in
\hyperref[key]{key polynomials} using a flagged version of the
\hyperref[rsk]{RSK correspondence}.  Since keys are atom-positive, the complete
flagged homogeneous polynomials are also \hyperref[atom]{Demazure
atom}-positive.  Conversely, key polynomials have a signed expansion in the
complete flagged homogeneous basis.


\section[atom]{Demazure atoms}

\begin{polydata}{atom}
  Name   & Demazure atoms \\
  Space    & All \\
  Basis    & True \\
  Rating   & 4 \\
  Bib      & Lascoux1990Keys \\
  Year     & 1990 \\
  Symbol   & $\atom_{\lambda,\sigma}(z)$ \\
  Keywords & divided-difference, fillings, polytopes, vertex model \\
  Category & Schur \\
  PositiveIn & monomial | Mason2009 | scope=nonsymmetric monomials; proof=semistandard augmented fillings \\
  PositiveIn & fundamentalParticle | Searles2019PolynomialBases,HicksNiese2024x | proof=Thm. 3.14 and Cor. 3.11 \\
\end{polydata}

The Demazure atoms (originally called \defin{standard bases}\label{standardBasis})
may be defined in a manner similar to \hyperref[keyDefinitionOperators]{key polynomials} using operators.
They were introduced by \name{Alain Lascoux} and \name{Marcel-Paul Schützenberger} in \cite{Lascoux1990Keys}.
Mason's semistandard augmented filling model gives a positive monomial
expansion \cite{Mason2009}.
They also expand positively in the
\hyperref[fundamentalParticle]{fundamental particle basis}
\cite{Searles2019PolynomialBases,HicksNiese2024x}.
The sourced skew analogue recorded here is the
\hyperref[keySkew]{skew key polynomial}, rather than a separate skew Demazure
atom family.
\name{Ben Brubaker}, \name{Valentin Buciumas}, \name{Daniel Bump}, and
\name{Henrik P. A. Gustafsson} construct solvable colored five-vertex lattice
models whose partition functions are type $A$ Demazure atoms
\cite{BrubakerBuciumasBumpGustafsson2021}.  Their proof uses a colored
Yang--Baxter equation, and the construction also gives algorithms for computing
Lascoux--Schützenberger keys.
Using the notation for divided difference operators, let $\theta_i \coloneqq \pi_i -1$.
These satisfy the braid relations and we have $\theta_i \theta_j = \theta_j \theta_i$
whenever $|i-j| \geq 2$.  However, $\theta_i^2 = 0$,
so we must now pay extra attention to choices of permutations.

Let $\lambda$ be a partition with at most $n$ parts, and let $\sigma \in \symS_n$
be a minimal coset representative in $\symS_n / Stab_n(\lambda)$.
The \defin{Demazure atom}\label{demazureAtom} $\atom_{\lambda,\sigma}(z)$ is
defined as
\begin{equation*}
 \atom_{\lambda,\sigma}(z) \coloneqq \theta_\sigma\left( z_1^{\lambda_1} \dotsm z_n^{\lambda_n} \right).
\end{equation*}
Again, instead of using $(\lambda,\sigma)$ as an index, we use
$\sigma(\lambda)$ as an index.
Here, we must choose $\sigma$ to be the \hyperref[reducedWord]{shortest permutation} $\sigma$
that sends $\lambda$ to $\alpha = \sigma(\lambda)$.

For example, if $\alpha = 1113$, then $\lambda=3111$,
and we must pick $\sigma = 4123$, and
\[
\atom_{1113}(z) = \atom_{3111,4123}(z) =
z_1 z_2 z_3 z_4^3 +
z_1 z_2 z_3^2 z_4^2 +
z_1 z_2^2 z_3z_4^2 +
z_1^2 z_2 z_3 z_4^2.
\]
Similarly,
\[
 \atom_{103}(z) = \atom_{310,312}(z) = z_1^3 z_2  + z_1^3 z_3  + z_1^2 z_2^2 +  z_1^2 z_3^2 +  z_1^2 z_2 z_3 +
  z_1 z_2^3 + z_1 z_3^3 + z_1 z_2 z_3^2 + z_1 z_2^2 z_3.
\]

Since $\pi_i = \theta_i+1$, it follows that key polynomials are
positive in the Demazure atom basis.
Indeed, \cite[Thm.~2.8]{Pun2016Thesis} gives
\[
	\key_{\alpha}(z) = \sum_{\beta \leq \alpha} \atom_{\beta}(z),
\]
where we have $\beta \leq \alpha$ if one can obtain
$\beta$ from $\rev(\alpha)$ by successively permuting entries which are
in increasing order.
For example,
\[
 \key_{301} = \atom_{103} + \atom_{130}+\atom_{301}+\atom_{310}
\]
since $103$, $130$, $301$ and $310$ can be obtained from $\rev(301)=103$
by successive swaps of elements (see also \hyperref[bruhatOrder]{Bruhat order}).
Similarly,
\[
\key_{002} = \atom_{200} + \atom_{020} + \atom_{002}.
\]
\emph{The notation used here is not uniform in the literature.}

\begin{example*}[Key polynomials into atoms]
\[
 \key_{202} = \atom_{202}+\atom_{220}
\]
and
\[
 \key_{301} = \atom_{103} + \atom_{130}+\atom_{301}+\atom_{310}
\]
\end{example*}

See \name{Anna Pun}'s PhD thesis for a good overview of Demazure atoms and
key polynomials \cite{Pun2016Thesis}.

\name{Sam Armon} studies atom-positivity through Demazure crystals
\cite{Armon2023x}.  The paper gives a recursive formula for crystal
Demazure atoms, generalizing a result of Lascoux--Schützenberger, and
separates the notions of extremal subsets and atom-positive subsets in the
crystal setting.


\subsection[atomCauchyKernel]{Cauchy kernel}

A bijective proof of the Cauchy-type identity below is given in
\cite[Thm.~6]{Lascoux2003}.
The proof uses the \hyperref[rsk]{Robinson--Schensted--Knuth correspondence}.

\begin{theorem}[See \cite[Thm.~6]{Lascoux2003}]
We have
\[
\prod_{i+j \leq n+1} (1-x_iy_j)^{-1} =
\sum_{\alpha \in \setN^n} \atom_{w_0\alpha}(\xvec) \key_{\alpha}(\yvec).
\]
\end{theorem}

In \cite{FuLascoux2009}, the authors prove this formula and several other Cauchy-type
identities involving key polynomials and Demazure atoms in other types.

A generalization to \emph{truncated staircase shapes} is provided
by \name{Olga Azenhas} and \name{Aram Emami} in \cite{AzenhasEmami2015}.
They use a version of RSK introduced by \name{Sarah Mason} in \cite{Mason2008}.

\name{Evgeny Feigin}, \name{Anton Khoroshkin}, and
\name{Ievgen Makedonskyi} \cite{FeiginKhoroshkinMakedonskyi2024x}
extend this circle of Cauchy identities from triangular matrices to arbitrary
staircase-shaped matrices.  Their standard filtrations give positive Cauchy
identities whose terms are products of key polynomials and Demazure atoms, as
well as an alternating-sign identity involving products of key polynomials.

\name{Evgeny Feigin}, \name{Ievgen Makedonskyi}, and \name{Daniel Orr}
interpret the $t=0$ specialization of the Mimachi--Noumi nonsymmetric
$q$-Cauchy identity representation-theoretically \cite{FeiginMakedonskyiOrr2023x}.
They realize the product side as a character of functions on a matrix space and
identify the associated graded pieces with tensor products of generalized
global Weyl modules for the Iwahori algebra.

An alternative proof also appears in \cite{ChoiKwon2017} where the
authors use $U_q(\mathfrak{g})$ \hyperref[crystals]{crystals}.

See also \cite{AzenhasGobetLecouvey2022x} for the connection between the Cauchy kernel,
\hyperref[rsk]{RSK}, \hyperref[crystals]{crystals} and last passage percolation.


\begin{example}[Personal communication, Vasu Tewari 2019]
Recall the Cauchy identity for
\hyperref[schubert]{Schubert polynomials} (see e.g.
\cite[p.~30]{PostnikovStanley2008}).
\[
 \sum_{w \in \symS_n} \schubert_w(\xvec) \schubert_{w w_0}(\yvec) = \prod_{i+j \leq n} (1 + x_iy_j) =
 \prod_{k=1}^{n-1}\sum_{j=0}^k y_{n-k}^{k-j} \elementaryE_{j}(x_1,\dotsc,x_k).
\]
Since the \hyperref[schubert]{Schubert polynomials} are positive in the key basis,
it follows that the \hyperref[schubertAsSumOfElementary]{standard elementary monomials}
are key-positive.
\end{example}




\section[keysAndAtomsTypeBCD]{Demazure keys and Demazure atoms in type $B$, $C$ and $D$}


In \cite{FuLascoux2009}, \name{Amy Fu} and \name{Alain Lascoux} define
divided difference operators that allow us to define key polynomials and
Demazure atoms in types $B$, $C$ and $D$.

Let $s_i$ interchange $x_i$ and $x_{i+1}$ for $1 \leq i < n$, let $s_n$
send $x_n$ to $x_n^{-1}$, and let $\tau_n$ send
$(x_{n-1},x_n)$ to $(x_n^{-1},x_{n-1}^{-1})$.  Fu and Lascoux write
operators on the right.  With $f^w$ denoting the action of $w$ on $f$,
their isobaric divided differences are
\[
\begin{aligned}
f\pi_i
  &= \frac{x_i f-x_{i+1}f^{s_i}}{x_i-x_{i+1}},
    &&1 \leq i < n,\\
f\pi_n^C
  &= \frac{x_n f-x_n^{-1}f^{s_n}}{x_n-x_n^{-1}},\\
f\pi_n^B
  &= \frac{x_n f-f^{s_n}}{x_n-1},\\
f\pi_n^D
  &= \frac{f-x_{n-1}^{-1}x_n^{-1}f^{\tau_n}}
          {1-x_{n-1}^{-1}x_n^{-1}}.
\end{aligned}
\]
They also use the second family
$\widehat{\pi}_i\coloneqq\pi_i-1$ and
$\widehat{\pi}_n^\heartsuit\coloneqq\pi_n^\heartsuit-1$, for
$\heartsuit=B,C,D$.  The $\pi$-operators give the type $B$, $C$, and
$D$ Demazure characters by applying products of operators to dominant
monomials, while the hatted operators give the corresponding atom-like
family \cite[Sec.~3]{FuLascoux2009}.

In particular, the type $C$ key polynomials contain the
\hyperref[schurSymplectic]{symplectic Schur functions}.
There are operator, crystal-graph, and key-tableau definitions of these polynomials.

See also \cite{RamYip2011}, since key polynomials are specializations of
nonsymmetric Macdonald polynomials.
