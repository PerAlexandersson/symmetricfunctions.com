\section[quasiSymmetricFunctions]{Quasisymmetric functions}

Quasisymmetric functions were formally introduced by \name{Ira Gessel} in
\cite{Gessel1984}.
Earlier work on \hyperref[pPartition]{$P$-partitions} anticipated this
development.
For an introduction to quasisymmetric functions, see \cite{LuotoEtAl2013IntroQSymSchur}.


A function $f$ is \defin{quasisymmetric}\label{quasisymmetricFunctionDefinition}
if for every \hyperref[compositions]{composition} $\alpha$ of length $\ell$,
the coefficient of $x_1^{\alpha_1} \dotsm x_\ell^{\alpha_\ell}$
is the same as the coefficient of
$x_{i_1}^{\alpha_1} \dotsm x_{i_\ell}^{\alpha_\ell}$,
for any $0 \lt i_1 \lt i_2 \lt \dotsb \lt i_{\ell}$.
The set of quasisymmetric functions forms a graded ring, $\spaceQSym$.


Quasisymmetric functions of degree $n$ are usually indexed by either
\hyperref[compositions]{integer compositions} of $n$, or subsets of $[n-1]$.
Given a composition $\alpha \vDash n$ with $\ell$ parts, define
\[
S_\alpha
\coloneqq
\{\alpha_1, \alpha_1+\alpha_2,\dotsc,
\alpha_1+\alpha_2+\dotsb+\alpha_{\ell-1}\}.
\]
The bijection $\alpha \mapsto S_\alpha$ maps compositions of $n$ to subsets of
$[n-1]$.
The partial order $\alpha \leq \beta$ denotes
\defin{refinement}\label{compositionRefinementOrder}.  That is, $\beta$ can be
obtained from $\alpha$ by adding consecutive parts of $\alpha$.
When $\alpha \leq \beta$, we illustrate this relationship with bars between
parts of $\alpha$, such that parts between bars add to parts of $\beta$.

\begin{example*}[Refinement and bars]
Taken from \cite{AlexanderssonSulzgruber2019}.
\[
112|341|21|34|2
\quad\text{corresponds to}\quad
\alpha = 11234121342,
\quad
\beta = 48372.
\]
\end{example*}


The most prevalent quasisymmetric functions are perhaps the \hyperref[gessel]{Gessel quasisymmetric functions}.
\name{Philippe Nadeau} and \name{Vasu Tewari} study the interaction between
quasisymmetric polynomials and Postnikov's divided symmetrization operator
\cite{NadeauTewari2021Divided}.  Their formulas are motivated by Schubert
calculus and use the Aval--Bergeron--Bergeron decomposition modulo positive
degree quasisymmetric polynomials.
\name{Nantel Bergeron}, \name{Lucas Gagnon}, \name{Philippe Nadeau},
\name{Hunter Spink}, and \name{Vasu Tewari} introduce equivariant
\defin{equivariant quasisymmetry}\label{equivariantQuasisymmetry} for
polynomials in two alphabets
\cite{BergeronGagnonNadeauSpinkTewari2025x}.  This leads to double
fundamental and double forest polynomials, intended as quasisymmetric
analogues of double Schur and double Schubert polynomials.
\name{Ron M. Adin}, \name{Ira M. Gessel}, \name{Victor Reiner}, and
\name{Yuval Roichman} introduce
\defin{cyclic quasisymmetric functions}\label{cyclicQuasisymmetricFunction}
\cite{AdinGesselReinerRoichman2021}.  These are quasisymmetric functions
with compatibility under cyclic rotation of the variables, and the paper
develops cyclic analogues of descent-set enumerators and related bases.
\name{Xing Gao}, \name{Li Guo}, and \name{Xiao-Song Peng} introduce
\defin{multi-quasisymmetric functions}\label{multiQuasisymmetricFunction}
with semigroup exponents
\cite{GaoGuoPeng2024x}.  Their construction extends the usual
quasisymmetric Hopf-algebra framework and interacts with Rota--Baxter algebra
structures.

\section[qsymNSymDuality]{Duality with noncommutative symmetric functions}

The algebra $\spaceQSym$ is graded-dual to the algebra
\hyperref[nonComFunctions]{NSym} of noncommutative symmetric functions
\cite{GelfandKrobLascouxLeclercRetakhThibon1995}.
Under the standard pairing,
\[
 \langle \completeH_\alpha,\qmonom_\beta\rangle=\delta_{\alpha,\beta},
\]
where $\completeH_\alpha$ denotes the noncommutative complete homogeneous
basis and $\qmonom_\beta$ denotes the monomial quasisymmetric basis.
Thus product rules in $\spaceQSym$ are dual to coproduct rules in NSym, and
coproduct rules in $\spaceQSym$ are dual to products in NSym.

This duality should be compared with the ordinary Hall inner product on
\hyperref[symmetricFunctionsSpace]{symmetric functions}.  In the symmetric
world, Schur functions are self-dual.  In the QSym--NSym world, the two sides
are different algebras: composition-indexed quasisymmetric functions live on
the commutative side, while noncommutative complete, ribbon, immaculate, and
noncommutative Schur functions live on the dual side.

The most useful dictionary is:
\[
\begin{array}{lll}
\toprule
\text{QSym basis} & \text{Dual NSym basis} & \text{What it records}\\
\midrule
\qmonom_\alpha & \completeH_\alpha
  & \text{ordered blocks of a composition}\\
\gessel_\alpha & R_\alpha
  & \text{descent sets and ribbon shapes}\\
\text{dual immaculate} & \text{immaculate}
  & \text{composition-tableau Schur analogues}\\
\qPsi_\alpha,\qPhi_\alpha & \Psi_\alpha,\Phi_\alpha
  & \text{power-sum analogues}\\
\bottomrule
\end{array}
\]
Here \(R_\alpha\) denotes the noncommutative ribbon basis.  The first row is
the defining pairing, while the second row is often the practical one: many
combinatorial generating functions naturally expand in the fundamental
functions \(\gessel_\alpha\), and their dual statements use ribbons in NSym.

\begin{example}
For compositions of $2$, the duality gives
\[
 \langle \completeH_{2},\qmonom_{2}\rangle=1,\qquad
 \langle \completeH_{2},\qmonom_{11}\rangle=0,\qquad
 \langle \completeH_{11},\qmonom_{11}\rangle=1.
\]
The refinement order appears when changing from monomial quasisymmetric
functions to the \hyperref[gessel]{fundamental basis}; dually, the same
incidence matrix changes the noncommutative complete basis to the ribbon
basis.
\end{example}

The representation-theoretic shadow of this duality is the
\hyperref[zeroHeckeAlgebra]{0-Hecke algebra}.  The Grothendieck group of
finite-dimensional $0$-Hecke modules identifies with $\spaceQSym$, while the
Grothendieck group of projective modules identifies with NSym
\cite{KrobThibon1997}.  Under the quasisymmetric characteristic, simple
$0$-Hecke modules map to the fundamental quasisymmetric functions.
Induction of modules corresponds to multiplication, and restriction
corresponds to coproduct.  This is the direct analogue of how the Frobenius
characteristic packages induction and restriction for symmetric-group
representations.


\section[qmonom]{Monomial quasisymmetric functions}

\begin{polydata}{qmonom}

  Symbol   & $\qmonom_\alpha(\xvec)$ \\
  Year     & 1984 \\

  PositiveIn & monomial | Gessel1984 | scope=nonsymmetric monomials; proof=definition \\
\end{polydata}

Given a composition $\alpha$ with $\ell$ parts,
we define the \defin{monomial quasisymmetric functions}
\label{monomialQuasisymmetricFunctionDefinition} as
\[
\qmonom_\alpha(\xvec) \coloneqq 
\sum_{i_1 < i_2 < \dotsb < i_\ell} 
x_{i_1}^{\alpha_1} x_{i_2}^{\alpha_2} \dotsm x_{i_\ell}^{\alpha_\ell}.
\]
The functions $\qmonom_\alpha$ constitute a basis for the space of
homogeneous quasisymmetric functions of degree $n$ as $\alpha$ ranges over all
compositions of $n$.

The monomial quasisymmetric functions refine the \hyperref[monomial]{monomial symmetric functions},
\[
\monomial_{\lambda}(\xvec) = \sum_{\alpha \sim \lambda}\qmonom_{\alpha}(\xvec)
\]
where we sum over all compositions $\alpha$ that are a permutation of $\lambda$.

\name{Oliver Pechenik} and \name{Matthew Satriano} introduce double monomial
quasisymmetric functions as torus-equivariant Schubert representatives for the
James reduced product model of $\spaceQSym$ \cite{PechenikSatriano2023x}.
They give a cellular basis and a combinatorial Littlewood--Richardson rule for
the structure constants.

\name{Darij Grinberg} and \name{Ekaterina A. Vassilieva} construct an
enriched monomial basis of $\spaceQSym$ from weighted posets
\cite{GrinbergVassilieva2021}.  This gives another bridge between
\hyperref[pPartition]{$P$-partition} enumerators and quasisymmetric bases.

There are two quasisymmetric refinements of the
\hyperref[powerSum]{power-sum symmetric functions} introduced in
\cite{BallantineDaughertyHicksMason2020}, denoted $\qPsi_\alpha$ and
$\qPhi_\alpha$.

\name{Anthony Lazzeroni} introduces another powersum basis for
quasisymmetric functions, defined by matrix fillings
\cite{Lazzeroni2023}.  It has a shuffle product, a deconcatenation coproduct,
and a change-of-basis rule to the fundamental quasisymmetric functions using
tuples of ribbons.  The construction also lifts to quasisymmetric functions in
non-commuting variables.
\name{John M. Campbell} uses quasisymmetric power sums to define
quasi-immanants \cite{Campbell2025x}.  These refine immanants by replacing
cycle types with cycle compositions, and a special case attached to
\hyperref[qSchur]{quasisymmetric Schur functions} gives an analogue of
second immanants.


Given a pair of compositions of $n$, $\alpha \leq \beta$, related by
\begin{equation*}
 \alpha_{11} \alpha_{12} \dotsc \alpha_{1,i_1}|
 \alpha_{21} \alpha_{22} \dotsc \alpha_{2,i_2} | \dotsb |
 \alpha_{k1} \alpha_{k2} \dotsc \alpha_{k,i_k}
\end{equation*}
let 
\begin{equation*}
\pi(\alpha,\beta)
\coloneqq
\prod_{j=1}^k
(\alpha_{j1})(\alpha_{j1}+\alpha_{j2})\dotsb
(\alpha_{j1}+\alpha_{j2}+\dotsb +\alpha_{j,i_j}).
\end{equation*}
The \defin{quasisymmetric power sum}\label{quasisymmetricPowerSumPsi}
$\qPsi_\alpha$ is defined as
\begin{equation*}
\qPsi_\alpha(\xvec)
\coloneqq
z_\alpha
\sum_{\beta \geq \alpha}
\frac{1}{\pi(\alpha,\beta)} \qmonom_\beta(\xvec).
\end{equation*}
For example,
$\Psi_{231} =
\frac{1}{10}\qmonom_6 +
\frac{1}{4}\qmonom_{24}+
\frac{3}{5}\qmonom_{51}+\qmonom_{231}$.
It was shown in \cite{BallantineDaughertyHicksMason2020}
that quasisymmetric power sums refine the usual power sums as
\[
\powerSum_{\lambda}(\xvec) = \sum_{\alpha \sim \lambda}\qPsi_{\alpha}(\xvec).
\]


The $\qPsi_\alpha$ have a nice relationship with \hyperref[gesselQuasiPowerSum]{Gessel quasisymmetric functions}.

\name{Seung-Il Choi}, \name{Young-Hun Kim}, and \name{Young-Tak Oh}
\cite{ChoiKimOh2024} study quasisymmetric power-sum expansions through
poset modules for the $0$-Hecke algebra.  Their method applies to several
Schur-like quasisymmetric bases, including the \hyperref[diSchur]{dual
immaculate} and \hyperref[extSchur]{extended Schur} bases, and gives
border-strip-tableau descriptions for the coefficients in these
$\qPsi$-expansions.

In \cite{AliniaeifardWangWilligenburg2021x}, the authors introduce
a third quasisymmetric refinement of the power-sum basis, denoted $\qRho_\alpha$.
They refer to this basis as the
\defin{combinatorial quasisymmetric power-sum basis}
\label{combinatorialQuasisymmetricPowerSumBasis}.

\name{Ricky Ini Liu} and \name{Michael Tang} study shuffle bases of
quasisymmetric functions and use infinitesimal characters to characterize
when such bases give Hopf-algebra isomorphisms with the shuffle algebra of
compositions \cite{LiuTang2023x}.  Their framework gives general
constructions of quasisymmetric power sums, recovering several earlier
bases in a uniform way.
\name{Michael Tang} studies substring-compatible permutation statistics,
constructing a \defin{substring coalgebra}\label{substringCoalgebra}
analogous to the shuffle algebra of a
shuffle-compatible statistic \cite{Tang2025x}.  Under a weak shuffle
compatibility hypothesis, the shuffle algebra and substring coalgebra combine
to form a Hopf algebra; the paper conjectures that the descent, peak, and
valley sets are the only nontrivial statistics satisfying both compatibilities.

\begin{example*}[Substring coalgebra for descents]
The \hyperref[descentSet]{descent set} is substring-compatible.
If $\pi=\pi_1\dotsm\pi_n$ and
$\sigma=\pi_i\dotsm\pi_j$ is a consecutive substring, then $\DES(\sigma)$ is
obtained from $\DES(\pi)\cap\{i,\dotsc,j-1\}$ by subtracting $i-1$ from each
position.

For example, in the substring coalgebra for the descent statistic,
\[
\begin{aligned}
\Delta([213]_{\DES})
&= [\,]_{\DES}\otimes [213]_{\DES}
 + [2]_{\DES}\otimes [13]_{\DES} \\
&\quad + [21]_{\DES}\otimes [3]_{\DES}
 + [213]_{\DES}\otimes [\,]_{\DES}.
\end{aligned}
\]
Under the Hopf isomorphism $[ \pi]_{\DES}\mapsto
\gessel_{\operatorname{comp}(\DES(\pi))}$, this becomes the usual coproduct
\[
\Delta(\gessel_{(1,2)})
= 1\otimes\gessel_{(1,2)}
  + \gessel_{(1)}\otimes\gessel_{(2)}
  + \gessel_{(1,1)}\otimes\gessel_{(1)}
  + \gessel_{(1,2)}\otimes 1.
\]
\end{example*}

\name{Olivier Bouillot}, \name{Jean-Christophe Novelli}, and
\name{Jean-Yves Thibon} construct new bases of $\spaceQSym$ and
$\mathrm{WQSym}$
from a two-parameter deformation of the quasi-shuffle
\cite{BouillotNovelliThibon2022x}.  The deformation is defined using the
formal group law associated with the exponential generating function of the
homogeneous Eulerian polynomials, and the product rule in the new bases is
given by this operation.

\name{Hugo Mlodecki} constructs an explicit bidendriform automorphism of the
Hopf algebra of word quasisymmetric functions $\mathrm{WQSym}$
\cite{Mlodecki2024}.  The construction uses decompositions of packed words
through red and blue biplane forests and gives a concrete self-duality map
compatible with the bidendriform structure.

\name{Nantel Bergeron}, \name{Kelvin Chan}, \name{Farhad Soltani}, and
\name{Mike Zabrocki} study quasisymmetric polynomials in anticommuting
variables \cite{BergeronChanSoltaniZabrocki2023}.  The quotient of the
exterior algebra by the ideal generated by these quasisymmetric polynomials
has a ballot-sequence basis and Hilbert series
\[
  \sum_{k=0}^{\lfloor n/2\rfloor} f^{(n-k,k)} q^k.
\]

Let $\alpha$ be a composition and let $\lambda$
be the partition obtained from $\alpha$ by rearranging the parts in decreasing
order.
The monomial expansion of $\qRho_\alpha$ is given by
\[
\qRho_\alpha(\xvec) = \sum_{\beta} R_{\alpha \beta} \qmonom_\beta(\xvec)
\]
where $R_{\alpha \beta}$ is the number of
\hyperref[setPartitions]{ordered set partitions} (see \oeis{A000670})
\[
 \gamma_1 | \gamma_2 | \dotsb | \gamma_k
\]
with the following two properties. The word
\[
\lambda_{\gamma_{11}}, \lambda_{\gamma_{12}},\dotsc,\;
\lambda_{\gamma_{21}}, \lambda_{\gamma_{22}},\dotsc,\; \dotsc,
\lambda_{\gamma_{k1}}, \lambda_{\gamma_{k2}},\dotsc,
\]
is equal to $\alpha$, and
$
\lambda_{\gamma_{i1}}+ \lambda_{\gamma_{i2}}+\dotsb = \beta_i
$
for all $i$. For example, if $\lambda = 322211$, then
the set-partition $235|16|4$ contributes to $R_{\alpha \beta}$ where
\[
\alpha = (2,2,1,3,1,2), \qquad \beta = (5,4,2).
\]
The original article \cite{AliniaeifardWangWilligenburg2021x}
uses the enumeration of certain matrices to define the $R_{\alpha \beta}$,
but the above definition is equivalent to theirs.

\begin{array}{ll}
\toprule

\end{array}
\end{example*}

The authors also give formulas for computing product and coproduct.


\section[standardQuasiInvolution]{Involutions on quasisymmetric functions}

The \hyperref[standardInvolution]{standard involution on symmetric functions}
that sends $\schurS_\lambda$ to $\schurS_{\lambda'}$
can be extended to quasisymmetric functions by $\omega$.
Two other useful involutions, $\rho$ and $\psi$, are also defined via the
\hyperref[gessel]{Gessel quasisymmetric functions}
\cite{LuotoMykytiukWilligenburg2013}:
\[
\omega \gessel_{n,S}(x)
  = \gessel_{n,[n-1]\setminus (n-S)}(x), \qquad
\rho \gessel_{n,S}(x)
  = \gessel_{n,n-S}(x), \qquad
\psi \gessel_{n,S}(x)
  = \gessel_{n, [n-1]\setminus S}(x).
\]
Here, $n-S$ is the set $\{n-s : s \in S\}$.
Equivalently, $\rho\gessel_\alpha=\gessel_{\alpha^r}$, where $\alpha^r$
is the reverse composition.
The map $\omega$ restricts to the usual involution on $\spaceSym$, while
$\psi$ is the complementary descent-set involution.  These maps satisfy
$\psi=\rho\circ\omega=\omega\circ\rho$.

In \cite{BallantineDaughertyHicksMason2020}, it is shown that
$
\omega\left( \qPsi_\alpha \right) = (-1)^{|\alpha|-\length(\alpha)}\qPsi_{\alpha^r},
$
where $\alpha^r$ denotes the reverse of $\alpha$.
The same property holds for $\qRho_\alpha$, see
\cite[Thm.~5.7]{AliniaeifardWangWilligenburg2021x}.
