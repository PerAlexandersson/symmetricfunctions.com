LLT polynomials were originally introduced by \name{Alain Lascoux}, \name{Bernard Leclerc} and \name[J.Y. Thibon]{Jean-Yves Thibon} in \cite{Lascoux97ribbontableaux}.
It is a large family of symmetric functions, and can be seen as a $q$-deformation of products of \hyperref[schurSkew]{skew Schur functions}.
This family contains the usual Schur polynomials, the skew Schur functions
and the family of \hyperref[hallLittlewoodT]{transformed Hall--Littlewood polynomials}.
The original motivation for LLT polynomials was to study certain \hyperref[plethysm]{plethysm} coefficients,
and a relationship with the \hyperref[fockSpace]{Fock space} representation of the quantum affine algebra $U_q(\widehat{\mathfrak{sl}_n})$.
There are generalizations to other types, for example by \name{Cédric Lecouvey} \cite{Lecouvey2009},
and by \name{Ian Grojnowski} and \name{Mark Haiman} \cite{GrojnowskiHaiman2006}.

The original definition expresses the LLT polynomials (called \defin{spin LLT polynomials})
as a sum over so called \hyperref[borderStripTableauxDefinition]{ribbon tableaux}, weighted with a \emph{spin} statistic.
The authors originally named them \defin{ribbon Schur functions}, but they are now referred to as LLT polynomials.
A supersymmetric version of LLT polynomials, (akin to \hyperref[hookSchur]{supersymmetric Schur functions}) has 
also been considered, see e.g., \cite{CurranFrechetteYostWolffZhangZhang2021x} for a \hyperref[latticeModel]{lattice model} generating 
such \defin{supersymmetric LLT polynomials}.
\name{Andrew Gitlin} and \name{David Keating} give a Yang--Baxter integrable
vertex model for supersymmetric LLT polynomials \cite{GitlinKeating2023}.
Their partition functions are essentially \name{Thomas Lam}'s super ribbon
functions and yield Cauchy-type identities for these polynomials.

In \cite{LeclercThibon2000} it is proved that the coefficients in the Schur basis of spin LLT polynomials 
are \emph{parabolic Kazhdan--Lusztig polynomials}.
For the connection with Kazhdan--Lusztig polynomials and the more representation-theoretical
connection, see \cite{LaniniRam2019}.

LLT polynomials have a prominent role in the study of \hyperref[diagonalHarmonics]{diagonal harmonics}.
See \cite{BlasiakHaimanMorsePunSeelinger2021x} for recent results in this area.


\subsection[flaggedLLT]{Flagged LLT polynomials}

\name{Jonah Blasiak}, \name{Mark Haiman}, \name{Jennifer Morse},
\name{Anna Pun}, and \name{George H. Seelinger}
\cite{BlasiakHaimanMorsePunSeelinger2025x} introduce
\defin{flagged LLT polynomials} $G_{\nu,\sigma}$.  These generalize ordinary
LLT polynomials in the same way that flagged Schur functions generalize Schur
functions, and they have both algebraic and tableau-sum definitions.

One striking feature is that flagged LLT polynomials interact well with a
nonsymmetric plethysm operator.  This gives positive expansions of modified
$r$-nonsymmetric Macdonald polynomials into flagged LLT polynomials.  The same
paper conjectures that flagged LLT polynomials themselves expand positively in
\hyperref[atom]{Demazure atoms}; this would strengthen the usual Schur
positivity story for symmetric LLT polynomials.


\subsection[lltDefinitionRibbons]{Definition (ribbons)}

The original definition is given in \cite{LeclercThibon2000}.
A modern reference is \cite{Iijima2013}.

Let $\SSYT^{(k)}(\lambda/\mu)$ be the set of $k$-ribbon tableaux of shape $\lambda/\mu$.
Then the \defin{spin LLT polynomial} is defined as
\[
\LLTG^{(k)}_{\lambda/\mu}(\xvec;q) \coloneqq \sum_{T \in \SSYT^{(k)}(\lambda/\mu)} \xvec_T q^{spin(T)}.
\]


\subsection[lltDefinitionTuples]{Definition (tuples)}

\name{Mikael Bylund} and \name{Mark Haiman} discovered an alternative way to model LLT polynomials.
These are indexed by $k$-tuples $\nuvec$ of skew Young diagrams. When $k=1$,
we recover a single skew Schur function.
The Bylund--Haiman model is described in \cite{HaglundHaimanLoehr2005} where it is proved that 
the two models are equivalent. The proof uses a version of the \hyperref[littlewoodMap]{Littlewood map},
which sends ribbon tableaux to tuples of \hyperref[prelimTableaux]{semistandard Young tableaux}.


Given a $k$-tuple of skew-shapes $\nuvec$, let 
\[
\SSYT(\nuvec) = \SSYT(\nuvec^1) \times \SSYT(\nuvec^2)\times \dotsm \times \SSYT(\nuvec^k)
\]
where $\SSYT(\lambda)$ is the set of skew \hyperref[prelimTableaux]{semistandard Young tableaux} of shape $\lambda$.
For $T = (T^1,T^2,\dotsc,T^k) \in \SSYT(\nuvec)$,
let $\xvec^T$ denote the product $\xvec_{T^1}\dotsm \xvec_{T^k}$ where $\xvec_{T^i}$
is the usual weight of the semistandard Young tableau $T^i$.
Given a cell $u = (r,c)$ (row, column) in a skew diagram, the \defin{content} of $u$ is defined as $c(u)\coloneqq c-r$. 

Entries $T^i(u) \gt T^j(v)$ in a tuple form an \defin{inversion} if
either 
\begin{itemize}
\item $i \lt j$  and $c(u) = c(v)$, or
\item $i \gt j$ and $c(u) = c(v)-1$.
\end{itemize}
Finally, we can define the \defin{LLT polynomial}
\[
\LLT_\nuvec(\xvec;q) = \sum_{T \in \SSYT(\nuvec)}  q^{\inv(T)} \xvec_T 
\]
where $\inv(T)$ is the total number of inversions appearing in $T$. 
When all shapes in $\nuvec$ consist of single boxes, 
we get the family of \hyperref[unicellularLLT]{unicellular LLT polynomials}.
Note that $\LLT_\nuvec(\xvec;1) = \prod_{i} \schurS_{\nuvec^i}(\xvec)$, a product of skew Schur polynomials.

The original spin LLT polynomials and the modern LLT polynomials are related as follows.
If $\nuvec$ is the $k$-tuple obtained as the $k$-quotient of $\lambda/\mu$, then 
there is some $e$, depending only on $\lambda/\mu$, such that
\[
q^e \LLT_\nuvec(\xvec;q^{-2}) =  \LLTG^{(k)}_{\lambda/\mu}(\xvec;q).
\]
See \cite[Prop. 6.17]{GrojnowskiHaiman2006}.


\subsection[lltVertexModel]{Vertex-model}

It has recently been shown that LLT polynomials can be realized as the partition functions of
certain colored fermionic vertex models, see \cite{CorteelGitlinKeatingMeza2020x} and \cite{AggarwalBorodinWheeler2023}.
For connection with fermionic vertex models, see \cite{BrubakerBuciumasBumpGustafsson2020}.
The same vertex-model framework also leads to coloured corner processes from
asymptotics of LLT polynomials \cite{AggarwalBorodinWheeler2024}.

\subsection[lltCauchy]{Cauchy Identity}

A Cauchy identity for the spin LLT polynomials was first given in \cite{Lam2005},
and a combinatorial proof is given in \cite{Leeuwen2005}.

In \cite{CorteelGitlinKeatingMeza2020x}, the authors describe a
Yang--Baxter integrable vertex model for LLT polynomials, and prove a Cauchy-identity.
Here, the sum is taken over $k$-tuples of integer partitions,
\[
  \sum_{\nuvec} \LLT_\nuvec(x_1,\dotsc,x_n;q) \LLT_{\nuvec^\textrm{rot}}(y_1,\dotsc,y_n;q) = 
  \prod_{i=1}^n \prod_{j=1}^n \prod_{m=0}^{k-1} \frac{1}{1-x_iy_j t^m }
\]
where $\nuvec^\textrm{rot}$ rotates each partition 180 degrees, and reverses the order of the tuple.


\subsection[lltFundamentalExpansion]{Fundamental quasisymmetric expansion}

It is fairly straightforward from the tuples definition to recover the 
expansion in the \hyperref[gessel]{fundamental quasisymmetric basis}. 
This is done in \cite{HaglundHaimanLoehr2005} and we have that
\[
\LLT_\nuvec(\xvec;q) = \sum_{T \in \SYT(\nuvec)} q^{\inv(T)} \gessel_{D(T)}(\xvec).
\]
The set $\SYT(\nuvec)$ consists of all fillings of $\nuvec$ using entries 
in $\setN$ so that each shape has increasing rows and columns. 
The descent set $D(T)$ is computed with respect to the reading word. 
That is, $i$ is a descent if $i+1$ appears before $i$ when reading the boxes of $\nuvec$ in the reading order.


\subsection[lltSchurPositivity]{Schur positivity}

It is proved in \cite{LeclercThibon2000} that $\LLT_\nuvec(\xvec;q)$ is Schur-positive 
whenever $\nuvec$ is a $k$-tuple of straight shapes. 
The proof uses the \hyperref[fockSpace]{Fock space} representation of $U_q(\widehat{\mathfrak{sl}_n})$, see 
\cite{Iijima2013} for some background. 

\name{Ian Grojnowski} and \name{Mark Haiman} prove in an unpublished preprint, \cite{GrojnowskiHaiman2006},
that all LLT polynomials $\LLT_\nuvec(\xvec;q)$ for arbitrary tuples of skew shapes, are Schur-positive.
They use \hyperref[heckeAlgebra]{Hecke algebras} and deep representation theory --- their proof does not give a
combinatorial formula for the Schur coefficients.
For a geometric approach to characters of Hecke algebras, including
LLT-related characters and Schur-positivity questions, see
\cite{AbreuNigro2025}.

Some partial progress on a combinatorial proof of Schur positivity is given in \cite{Roberts2013}.


The case when the tuple consists of $k\leq 3$ ribbons is considered by \name{Jonah Blasiak} \cite{Blasiak2016},
and an explicit Schur expansion is given. Note that the ribbons need not be connected.
This result is used to show that Macdonald polynomials indexed with shapes that have at most three columns,
are Schur-positive.


In \cite{Tom2020x}, \name{Foster Tom} gives a combinatorial formula for the Schur expansion 
of certain vertical-strip LLT polynomials, whenever the underlying unit-interval
graph is triangle-free; see the \hyperref[unicellularLLT]{page on
unicellular LLT polynomials}. \name{Foster Tom} uses a generalization of cocharge.
This generalizes an earlier result by \name{Per Alexandersson} and \name{Joakim Uhlin} \cite{AlexanderssonUhlin2020}.
\name{Foster Tom} also proves that a horizontal-strip LLT polynomial is
determined by its weighted graph \cite{Tom2023}.

\section[lltUnicellularPointer]{Unicellular LLT polynomials}

When all shapes in the tuple consist of single boxes, one obtains the
\hyperref[unicellularLLT]{unicellular LLT polynomials}.  They have a graph
coloring model using unit interval graphs, a shifted
$\elementaryE$-positive orientation formula, and a twin-manifold
representation-theoretic interpretation.
