The \defin{Lascoux polynomials} are a family of non-symmetric,
non-homogeneous polynomials, first introduced by \name{Alain Lascoux} in \cite{Lascoux2001}.
The set $\{ \lascoux_\alpha(x_1,\dotsc,x_n) \}_\alpha$ where $\alpha$
ranges over all compositions of length $n$, is a basis for $\setC[x_1,\dotsc,x_n]$.

The Lascoux polynomials are the K-theoretic analog of the \hyperref[key]{key polynomials},
and they generalize the \hyperref[grothendieckGrassmann]{Grothendieck polynomials}.
Similarly, there are Lascoux-atom polynomials, which are K-theoretical 
analogs of the \hyperref[atom]{Demazure atom polynomials}.

\emph{Note:} There seem to be other types of polynomials referred to as Lascoux polynomials,
e.g. \cite{BorziChenMotwaniVenturelloVodicka2021}.


\subsection[lascouxDefinition]{Definition}

We use the same notation as in the operator definition of \hyperref[key]{key polynomials}.
Define the $\beta$-versions of the divided difference operator $\partial_i$, and $\pi_i$:
\[
\partial_i^{(\beta)}(f) \coloneqq 
\partial_i(f + \beta x_{i+1} f)
\qquad 
\pi_i^{(\beta)}(f) \coloneqq \partial_i^{(\beta)}(x_i f).
\]
The \defin{Lascoux polynomial} is then defined as
\[
\lascoux^{(\beta)}_{\alpha}(\xvec) \coloneqq 
\begin{cases}
\xvec^{\alpha} & \text{ if $\alpha$ is a partition} \\
\pi_i^{(\beta)} 
\lascoux^{(\beta)}_{s_i \alpha}(\xvec)
& \text{ if $\alpha_i \lt \alpha_{i+1}$}.
\end{cases}
\]
Note that $\key_{\alpha}(\xvec) = \lascoux^{(0)}_{\alpha}(\xvec)$,
that is, at $\beta=0$ we recover a key polynomial.

The first combinatorial model for Lascoux polynomials was conjectured by \name{Cara Monical} in \cite{Monical2017},
where these are given as a sum over certain set-valued tableaux. 
They also provide a combinatorial formula involving set-valued \emph{skyline fillings} 
(terminology from \hyperref[macdonaldE]{non-symmetric Macdonald polynomials}).
A proof of this conjecture was later given in \cite[Thm. 4.1]{BuciumasScrimshawWeber2020}.

Another set-valued tableau formula is proved in \cite[Thm. 1.1]{ReinerYong2021}.
A set-valued tableau formula is also given in \cite[Thm. 3.16]{Yu2023}.
Here, the tableaux have composition shape, and the author argues that his formula 
is simpler, as it does not use the Lusztig involution.

In \cite{MonicalPechenikScrimshaw2018}, the question is raised if there is some type of K-theoretical
crystal structure for Lascoux polynomials. This seems to be answered in \cite{Yu2023}.

The Lascoux polynomials also have a diagram formula using
\defin{$K$-Kohnert diagrams}.  This was conjectured by
\name{Colleen Ross} and \name{Alexander Yong}, and proved by
\name{Jianping Pan} and \name{Tianyi Yu} via a weight-preserving
bijection with reverse set-valued tableaux
\cite[Thm.~2]{PanYu2023}.
The same $K$-Kohnert model leads to snow diagrams for determining the
top degree of a Lascoux polynomial \cite[Thms.~1.1 and~1.2]{PanYu2024}.
\name{Kelsey Hanser} and \name{Nicholas Mayers} study the corresponding
maximal-ghost problem for more general diagrams and prove the snow-diagram
formula for several additional diagram classes
\cite{HanserMayers2026}.

\begin{example*}[A small $K$-Kohnert expansion]
Let $\alpha=(0,2,1)$.
Pan and Yu list the eleven $K$-Kohnert diagrams of $\alpha$ explicitly in
\cite[Ex.~2.13]{PanYu2024}.  Translating their diagram weights gives
\[
\begin{split}
\lascoux^{(\beta)}_{(0,2,1)}
&=x_2^2x_3+x_1x_2x_3+x_1^2x_3+x_1x_2^2+x_1^2x_2\\
&\phantom{=}
  +\beta\left(2x_1x_2^2x_3+2x_1^2x_2x_3+x_1^2x_2^2\right)
  +\beta^2x_1^2x_2^2x_3 .
\end{split}
\]
The first line is the key-polynomial part, and the powers of $\beta$
record how many ghost cells were left by the $K$-Kohnert moves.
For the key diagram $D(\alpha)$, the snow-diagram algorithm places dark
clouds at $(3,1)$ and $(2,2)$, and snowflakes at $(1,1)$ and $(1,2)$.
Thus $\operatorname{rajcode}(\alpha)=(2,2,1)$ and
$\operatorname{raj}(\alpha)=5$, so the top monomial is
$x_1^2x_2^2x_3$, matching the final term above
\cite[Thm.~1.2]{PanYu2024}.
\end{example*}

\subsection[lascouxProperties]{Properties of Lascoux polynomials}


The \hyperref[schubert]{Schubert polynomials} expand positively into
\hyperref[key]{key polynomials}.
In the same manner, in \cite[Thm.~1.9]{ShimozonoYu2021},
\name{Mark Shimozono} and \name{Tianyi Yu} give a formula
for the expansion of Grothendieck polynomials into Lascoux polynomials,
thus proving an earlier conjecture by \name{Vic Reiner} and
\name{Alexander Yong} \cite{ReinerYong2021}.

\name{Gidon Orelowitz} and \name{Tianyi Yu} prove a product rule for
Lascoux polynomials and \hyperref[grothendieckStable]{stable Grothendieck
polynomials}.  Their tableau formula expands
$\lascoux_{\alpha}^{(\beta)} \grothendieckStable_w^{(\beta)}$
as a graded nonnegative sum of Lascoux polynomials
\cite[Thm.~1.3]{OrelowitzYu2023x}.  The proof uses the row analogue of
Hecke column insertion introduced by \name{Daoji Huang},
\name{Mark Shimozono}, and \name{Tianyi Yu}
\cite{HuangShimozonoYu2024}.

There are also strong connections between Lascoux polynomials and
\hyperref[schubert]{Schubert polynomials} at top degree.  The top
homogeneous components of Lascoux polynomials form a basis for a graded
algebra, and \name{Tianyi Yu} proves that they are related to Schubert
polynomials by a reverse-complement operator \cite[Thm.~3.6 and
Cor.~3.8]{Yu2025}.  This transfers the Schubert structure constants to
the top-Lascoux structure constants \cite[Thm.~4.2]{Yu2025}.

\name{Linus Setiabrata} and \name{Avery St. Dizier} give double
orthodontia formulas for double Grothendieck polynomials.  As an
application, they prove a Lascoux-positivity theorem for a reversed
specialization of double Schubert polynomials indexed by vexillary
permutations \cite[Cor.~1.3]{SetiabrataDizier2024x}; they conjecture
that the same positivity holds without the vexillary hypothesis.

\name{Ekaterina Presnova} and \name{Evgeny Smirnov} give a
Gelfand--Zetlin polytope model for Lascoux polynomials.  They construct
a cellular decomposition of a Gelfand--Zetlin polytope and express a
Lascoux polynomial as a weighted sum over cells lying in the relevant
union of dual Kogan faces \cite[Thms.~4.3.1--4.3.3]{PresnovaSmirnov2024}.
This extends the Gelfand--Zetlin model for key polynomials.

\name{Eric Marberg} and \name{Travis Scrimshaw} introduce shifted
$P$- and $Q$-analogues of key polynomials and their K-theoretic
analogues.  Their shifted $P$-Lascoux polynomials and atoms are
$\setN[\beta]$-linear combinations of ordinary Lascoux polynomials and
Lascoux atoms \cite[Prop.~3.21]{MarbergScrimshaw2026}, while their
stable shifted Grothendieck functions have positive finite expansions in
stable Grothendieck functions \cite[Thms.~3.28 and~3.41]{MarbergScrimshaw2026}.

\name{Cara Monical}, \name{Oliver Pechenik}, and \name{Dominic Searles}
introduce the \defin{quasiLascoux basis} and \defin{kaon basis} in
combinatorial $K$-theory
\cite{MonicalPechenikSearles2021}.  The quasiLascoux basis is a
$K$-theoretic deformation of the quasikey basis and a polynomial lift of
quasiGrothendieck polynomials; it expands positively into both glide and
Lascoux atom bases, while the Lascoux basis expands positively into it.
Kaons are $K$-theoretic deformations of fundamental particles, and both
glide polynomials and Lascoux atoms expand positively into the kaon basis.
\name{Laura Pierson} proves a conjecture of
\name{Cara Monical}, \name{Oliver Pechenik}, and \name{Dominic Searles}
on cancellation in two K-theoretic polynomial expansions
\cite{Pierson2025KTheoretic}.  The result says that certain alternating
sums obtained by specializing the Lascoux-atom--to--kaon and
quasiLascoux--to--glide transition coefficients at $\beta=-1$ are always
$0$ or $1$, and the proof is by a sign-reversing involution.

\subsection[lascouxAtomVertexModel]{A vertex model for Lascoux atoms}

In \cite{BuciumasScrimshawWeber2020}, the authors construct a 5-vertex model 
whose partition function is a Lascoux atom.
This is the first combinatorial model for the Lascoux-atoms.
