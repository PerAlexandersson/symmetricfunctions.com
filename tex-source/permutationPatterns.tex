\section[permutationPattern]{Permutation patterns}

A permutation $\pi \in \symS_n$ is said to \defin{contain} the pattern $\sigma \in \symS_k$,
if there is a subsequence of $\pi$ which is order-isomorphic to $\sigma$.
For example, $\pi = [1, 8, 5, 3, 7, 4, 6, 2]$ contains the pattern $[2,3,1]$
as the subsequence $3, 6, 2$ in $\pi$ have its elements in the same relative order.

\subsection[arrowPatterns]{Arrow patterns and shallow permutations}

Arrow patterns are variants of permutation patterns designed to remember some
information from cycle notation while still being checked through one-line
notation.  An arrow pattern consists of an ordinary pattern together with
arrows between labels, and an occurrence must realize both the usual
order-isomorphism and the corresponding arrows under the permutation map.

\name{Yosef Berman} and \name{Bridget Eileen Tenner} introduced arrow
patterns in their study of pattern-functions and shallow permutations
\cite{BermanTenner2022}.  Let $I(\pi)$ be the inversion number, let
$T(\pi)$ be the reflection length, and let
$D(\pi)=\sum_i |\pi_i-i|$ be the total displacement.  The
Diaconis--Graham inequalities say
\[
  I(\pi)+T(\pi) \leq D(\pi) \leq 2I(\pi).
\]
A permutation is \defin{shallow} if equality holds in the lower bound.
These permutations can be characterized by pattern avoidance conditions in
cycle form.  Shallow permutations avoiding a classical pattern of length
three were enumerated by \name{Kassie Archer}, \name{Aaron Geary}, and
\name{Robert P. Laudone} \cite{ArcherGearyLaudone2026}.

\name{Kassie Archer} and \name{Robert P. Laudone} initiated a systematic
study of arrow-pattern avoidance, including arrow-Wilf equivalence and
enumeration of several avoidance classes \cite{ArcherLaudone2026x}.
\name{Shishuo Fu} and \name{Zhenghe Yang} then studied simultaneous
avoidance of a classical pattern of length $3$ and an arrow pattern of
length $3$, resolving three conjectures of Archer--Laudone
\cite{FuYang2026x}.


\subsection[directSumPermutations]{Direct sums and skew sums of permutations}

Given $\pi \in \symS_m$ and $\sigma \in \symS_n$, we define the \defin{direct sum}
and the \defin{skew sum} of $\pi$ and $\sigma$ as
\begin{align}
 \pi \ast \sigma \coloneqq & [pi_1,\dotsc,\pi_m,\sigma_1+m,\dotsc,\sigma_n+m]  \in \symS_{m+n} \\
 \pi \times \sigma \coloneqq & [pi_1+n,\dotsc,\pi_m+n,\sigma_1,\dotsc,\sigma_n]  \in \symS_{m+n}
\end{align}
Note that these operations are not commutative, but they are associative.


\subsection[patternAvoidanceClasses]{Pattern-avoidance classes}

Many important families of permutations are defined by pattern avoidance.
Here are some of the most common ones:
\begin{itemize}
\item \defin{Dominant} (132-avoiding), counted by Catalan numbers \oeis{A000108}.
\item \hyperref[vexillary]{Vexillary} (2143-avoiding), see \oeis{A005802}.
\item \defin{Grassmannian} (at most one descent), see \oeis{A000325}.
\item \defin{Separable} (avoids $2413$ and $3142$), see \oeis{A006318}.
\item \defin{Baxter} (avoids $2\text{-}14\text{-}3$ and $3\text{-}41\text{-}2$).
\item \defin{Fully commutative} (321-avoiding), counted by Catalan numbers.
\end{itemize}
For a comprehensive list including non-pattern-based families,
see the \hyperref[namedPermutations]{named families of permutations} page.
