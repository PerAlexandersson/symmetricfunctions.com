Macdonald polynomials, $\{\macdonaldP_\lambda(\xvec;q,t) \}_{\lambda}$,
were introduced by \name{Ian Macdonald} in \cite{Macdonald1987}.  They are a
two-parameter extension of the Schur functions and unify the
\hyperref[jackP]{Jack polynomials} and
\hyperref[hallLittlewoodP]{Hall--Littlewood polynomials}.
For column partitions one has
$\macdonaldP_{(1^r)}(\xvec;q,t)=\elementaryE_r(\xvec)$.

The master's thesis by \name{Robin Langer} \cite{Langer2009} gives an overview.
\name{Laura Colmenarejo} and \name{Arun Ram} suggest the term
\defin{bosonic Macdonald polynomials}\label{bosonicMacdonaldPolynomial},
while the
\hyperref[macdonaldE]{nonsymmetric Macdonald polynomials}
are called
\defin{electronic Macdonald polynomials}\label{electronicMacdonaldPolynomial},
see \cite{ColmenarejoRam2022x}.
\name{Laura Colmenarejo}, \name{Charles F. Dunkl}, and
\name{Jean-Gabriel Luque} compare vector-valued and highest-weight Jack and
Macdonald polynomials \cite{ColmenarejoDunklLuque2019x}.  Their results
study when projections from vector-valued Jack or Macdonald polynomials
commute with the relevant symmetric-group, Hecke-algebra, and Cherednik
operator actions.

\name{Alice Guionnet} and \name{Jiaoyang Huang} study large-variable
asymptotics of skew Macdonald and Jack polynomials
\cite{GuionnetHuang2026}.  Their results characterize logarithmic limits by
variational problems, using a large-deviation principle for
$\theta$-analogues of non-intersecting Bernoulli random walks.  At
$\theta=1$ this recovers the lozenge-tiling variational picture of
\name{Henry Cohn}, \name{Richard Kenyon}, and \name{James Propp}.

\name{Sylvie Corteel}, \name{Olya Mandelshtam}, and \name{Lauren Williams}
give combinatorial formulas for Koornwinder polynomials, also known as
Macdonald polynomials of type $\tilde{C}$, in terms of rhombic staircase
tableaux \cite{CorteelMandelshtamWilliams2024}.  Their formulas also cover
open-boundary ASEP polynomials related to nonsymmetric Koornwinder
polynomials.
\name{Grigori Olshanski} relates Macdonald polynomials to an extended
Gelfand--Tsetlin graph \cite{Olshanski2021}.  This puts Macdonald-positive
kernels and coherent systems into the branching-graph framework.
\name{Jaeseong Oh} studies cyclic sieving phenomena built from Macdonald
polynomials \cite{Oh2022}.
\name{Shinji Koshida} studies observables of periodic Macdonald processes
using free field theory \cite{Koshida2021}.
\name{Rekha Biswal}, \name{Vyjayanthi Chari}, \name{Peri Shereen}, and
\name{Jeffrey Wand} define symmetric polynomials $G_{\nu,\lambda}(\xvec;q)$
whose $\lambda=0$ specialization is a specialized Macdonald polynomial and
whose $\nu=0$ specialization is the graded character of a level-two
Demazure module for affine $\mathfrak{sl}_{n+1}$
\cite{BiswalChariShereenWand2019x}.  They prove
\hyperref[schurPositiveDefinition]{Schur positivity} under
natural hypotheses and express the family through Macdonald polynomials.
\name{Jimmy He} constructs a characteristic map for the symmetric space of
symplectic forms over a finite field \cite{He2020}.  Under this map the
spherical functions are sent to Macdonald polynomials with parameters
$(q,q^2)$, and the construction gives
\hyperref[schurPositiveDefinition]{Schur-positivity} results for skew Macdonald
polynomials at these parameters.

The specialization $t=q^2$ is distinguished by He's characteristic map for
the symmetric space of symplectic forms over a finite field.  If $q$ is an odd
prime power, the Schur coefficient
\[
  \left\langle
    \macdonaldP_{\lambda/\mu}(\xvec;q,q^2),\schurS_\nu
  \right\rangle
\]
is nonnegative \cite[Thm.~0.1]{He2020}.  This is a numerical positivity
statement at finite-field values of $q$, not a claim that the coefficients are
polynomials in $q$ with nonnegative integer coefficients.

For example, the degree-two polynomial is
\[
  \macdonaldP_{(2)}(\xvec;q,t)
  =
  \schurS_{(2)}(\xvec)
  +
  \frac{q-t}{1-qt}\schurS_{(1,1)}(\xvec),
\]
and hence
\[
  \macdonaldP_{(2)}(\xvec;q,q^2)
  =
  \schurS_{(2)}(\xvec)
  +
  \frac{q}{1+q+q^2}\schurS_{(1,1)}(\xvec),
\]
which is positive for positive real $q$, in particular for odd prime powers.
The vector-valued Jack and Macdonald polynomials of
\cite{ColmenarejoDunklLuque2019x} live in a representation-valued setting;
here they are recorded as a projection and highest-weight comparison rather
than as a separate scalar symmetric-function basis.

\subsection[macdonaldPCreationCharacters]{Creation formula and Macdonald characters}

\name{Houcine Ben Dali} and \name{Michele D'Adderio} introduce an operator
$\Gamma$ which gives a creation formula for Macdonald polynomials
\cite{DaliDAdderio2024x}.  The formula connects the Bergeron--Garsia--
Haiman--Tesler operator viewpoint with shifted Macdonald polynomials, and is
used to define
\defin{two-parameter Macdonald characters}\label{macdonaldCharacter},
which generalize \hyperref[jackCharacters]{Jack characters}.  They also
formulate positivity conjectures extending Goulden--Jackson type conjectures
for Jack polynomials.


\subsection[macdonaldPUniqueDefinition]{Inner product characterization}

Let $\langle \cdot , \cdot \rangle_{q,t}$ denote the
\defin{Macdonald inner product}\label{macdonaldInnerProduct} on symmetric
functions such that
\[
\langle \powerSum_\lambda , \powerSum_\mu \rangle_{q,t}
=
z_\lambda \delta_{\lambda\mu}
\prod_{i=1}^{\length(\lambda)}
\frac{1-q^{\lambda_i}}{1-t^{\lambda_i}} .
\]
Note that at $q=t=0$, we recover the standard Hall inner product.

The \defin{Macdonald polynomials}\label{macdonaldPolynomialDefinition}
$\macdonaldP_\lambda(\xvec;q,t)$ are
defined as the unique family of polynomials
such that
\begin{equation*}
\macdonaldP_\lambda(\xvec;q,t) =
\monomial_\lambda(\xvec) + \sum_{\mu \prec \lambda}
\eta_{\lambda\mu} \monomial_\mu(\xvec)
\end{equation*}
and $\langle \macdonaldP_\lambda, \macdonaldP_\mu \rangle_{q,t}=0$
whenever $\lambda \neq \mu$.
Here, $\prec$ denotes the \hyperref[dominanceOrder]{dominance order on partitions}.

\begin{example*}[Macdonald polynomials for $\lambda \vdash 3$]
For degree $3$, the monomial expansions are
\begin{align*}
\macdonaldP_{111}(\xvec;q,t) &= \monomial_{111} \\
\macdonaldP_{21}(\xvec;q,t) &=
\monomial_{21}
+  \frac{(t-1) (2 q t+q+t+2)}{q t^2-1} \monomial_{111} \\
\macdonaldP_{3}(\xvec;q,t) &=
\monomial_{3}
+ \frac{\left(q^2+q+1\right)(t-1)}{q^2 t-1} \monomial_{21} \\
&\phantom{=}
+ \frac{(q+1) \left(q^2+q+1\right) (t-1)^2}
{(q t-1)\left(q^2 t-1\right)} \monomial_{111}
\end{align*}
\end{example*}

This characterization is not efficient for actual computation.  The
\hyperref[macdonaldPTableauFormula]{tableau formula} below is usually more practical.

\subsection[macdonaldPEigenvectors]{Eigenvector characterization}

The Macdonald polynomials can also be characterized as eigenvectors
to a certain \defin{Macdonald difference operator}\label{macdonaldDifferenceOperator}
$D$, see \cite{Macdonald1987}.
The operator $D$ is given as
\[
D
=
\sum_{i=1}^n
\prod_{j \neq i} \left( \frac{tx_i-x_j}{x_i-x_j} \right) T_{q,x_i}
\]
where
$T_{q,x_i} f(x_1,\dotsc,x_n)
= f(x_1,\dotsc,x_{i-1},qx_i,x_{i+1},\dotsc,x_n)$.

Macdonald polynomials can also be produced via determinants;
see \cite{LapointeLascouxMorse1998}.

\name{Philip Schlösser} studies
\defin{intermediate Macdonald polynomials}\label{intermediateMacdonaldPolynomial},
which are
invariant under a parabolic subgroup of the finite Weyl group and interpolate
between nonsymmetric and symmetric Macdonald polynomials
\cite{Schlosser2026Macdonald}.  The paper proves orthogonality, eigenfunction
properties, norm formulas, and vector-valued interpretations using
double-affine Hecke algebras.

\name{Luc Lapointe} introduces
\defin{$m$-symmetric functions}\label{mSymmetricFunction}, which are symmetric in
the variables $x_{m+1},x_{m+2},\dotsc$ but not necessarily in
$x_1,\dotsc,x_m$ \cite{Lapointe2022x}.  He constructs $m$-symmetric
Macdonald and Schur functions and formulates positivity conjectures for the
modified $m$-symmetric Macdonald polynomials.  \name{Manuel Concha} and
\name{Luc Lapointe} establish the basic structure of the $m$-symmetric
Macdonald polynomials, including orthogonality, norm, evaluation, inclusion,
and Cauchy-type formulas \cite{ConchaLapointe2023Macdonald}.  \name{Ben
Goodberry} studies the same family as type $A$
\defin{partially symmetric Macdonald polynomials}\label{partiallySymmetricMacdonaldPolynomial}
and proves stability, an integral form, and Pieri-type rules
\cite{Goodberry2025}.
\name{Daniel Orr} and \name[Milo B. Weising]{Milo Bechtloff Weising}
identify normalized fixed-point classes in parabolic flag Hilbert schemes
with modified partially symmetric Macdonald polynomials
\cite{OrrWeising2024x}.  This confirms the Goodberry--Orr conjectural
geometric interpretation and gives an explicit formula for the induced
involution on the polynomial representation.

\name{Manuel Concha} and \name{Luc Lapointe} study
\defin{bisymmetric Macdonald polynomials}\label{bisymmetricMacdonaldPolynomial}
\cite{ConchaLapointe2023x}.  These are built from nonsymmetric
Macdonald polynomials by combining antisymmetrization and $t$-symmetrization.
They prove an evaluation symmetry extending the usual Macdonald symmetry, and
derive Pieri rules for multiplication by elementary symmetric functions.



\subsection[macdonaldPTableauFormula]{Tableau formula}

The classical tableau formula expresses Macdonald polynomials as a sum over
tableaux; see \cite{Macdonald1987} and \cite{Macdonald1995}.
We first need to introduce some notation.
Given $\lambda/\mu$, let $R_{\lambda/\mu}$ and $C_{\lambda/\mu}$ be the set of
rows and columns, respectively, containing some box of $\lambda/\mu$.

When $\lambda/\mu$ is a \emph{horizontal strip} (no two boxes in the same column),
we define $\psi_{\lambda/\mu}(q,t)$ as the following product.
Set
\[
\psi_{\lambda/\mu}(q,t) \coloneqq
\prod_{\substack{ s \in R_{\lambda/\mu} \setminus C_{\lambda/\mu} }}
\frac{b_{\mu}(s;q,t)}{b_{\lambda}(s;q,t)}
\]
where
\[
b_{\mu}(s;q,t) \coloneqq
\begin{cases}
\frac{ 1-q^{\arm_\mu(s)}t^{1+\leg_\mu(s)}  }{ 1-q^{1+\arm_\mu(s)}t^{\leg_\mu(s)} }
&\text{if $s\in \mu$} \\
1 &\text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\]

\begin{example*}[Computation of $\psi_{\lambda/\mu}(q,t)$]
Let $\lambda=(8,5,5,1)$ and $\mu=(6,5,2)$.
It suffices to take the product over the boxes that lie both in $\mu$ and in
$R_{\lambda/\mu} \setminus C_{\lambda/\mu}$.
The three such boxes are $(1,2)$, $(1,6)$, and $(3,2)$, marked in the figure.
\begin{figure}
\begin{ytableau}
*(lightblue) & \ast & *(lightblue) & *(lightblue) & *(lightblue) & \ast & *(gray) & *(gray)\\
*(lightblue) & *(lightblue) & *(lightblue) & *(lightblue) & *(lightblue) \\
*(lightblue) & \ast &  *(gray) & *(gray)& *(gray) \\
*(gray)
\end{ytableau}
\end{figure}
We then have
\[
b_{\mu}((1,2);q,t) = \frac{1-q^{4}t^{1+2}}{1-q^{1+4}t^{2}} \qquad
b_{\lambda}((1,2);q,t) = \frac{1-q^{6}t^{1+2}}{1-q^{1+6}t^{2}}.
\]
\[
b_{\mu}((1,6);q,t) = \frac{1-q^{0}t^{1+0}}{1-q^{1+0}t^{0}} \qquad
b_{\lambda}((1,6);q,t) = \frac{1-q^{2}t^{1+0}}{1-q^{1+2}t^{0}}.
\]
\[
b_{\mu}((3,2);q,t) = \frac{1-q^{0}t^{1+0}}{1-q^{1+0}t^{0}} \qquad
b_{\lambda}((3,2);q,t) = \frac{1-q^{3}t^{1+0}}{1-q^{1+3}t^{0}}.
\]
In total, $\psi_{\lambda/\mu}(q,t)$ is given by
\[
\frac{(1-q^{4}t^{3})}{(1-q^{5}t^{2})}
\frac{(1-q^{7}t^{2})}{(1-q^{6}t^{3})}
\frac{(1-t)}{(1-q)}
\frac{(1-q^{3})}{(1-q^{2}t)}
\frac{(1-t)}{(1-q)}
\frac{(1-q^{4})}{(1-q^{3}t)}.
\]
\end{example*}

Now, if $T$ is a \hyperref[prelimTableaux]{semistandard Young tableau},
we let
\[
\psi_{T}(q,t)
\coloneqq
\prod_{j=1}^n \psi_{\lambda^{j}/\lambda^{j-1}}(q,t)
\]
where $\lambda^{j}/\lambda^{j-1}$ is the horizontal strip determined
by the entries with value $j$ in $T$.

The tableau formula is
\[
\macdonaldP_{\mu}(\xvec;q,t)
=
\sum_{\nu \vdash n} \monomial_\nu(\xvec)
\sum_{T \in \SSYT(\mu,\nu)}  \psi_{T}(q,t).
\]

Note that if $q=t$, this formula gives the Schur polynomial
$\schurS_\mu(\xvec)$.

\name{Olya Mandelshtam} gives a more compact tableau formula for the symmetric
Macdonald polynomials $\macdonaldP_\lambda(\xvec;q,t)$ using coquinv-sorted
non-attacking tableaux and probabilistic operators
\cite{Mandelshtam2024x}.  The formula has fewer terms than the classical
tableau formula and is connected by a bijection to multiline queues; in a
limit, it recovers the Knop--Sahi formula for Jack polynomials.


\subsection[macdonaldPFromE]{From nonsymmetric Macdonald $E$ polynomials}


\name{Jim Haglund}, \name{Mark Haiman}, and \name{Nicholas Loehr} obtain the
following expansion \cite[Prop.~5.3.1]{HaglundHaimanLoehr2008}:
\begin{equation*}
\macdonaldP_\lambda(\xvec;q,t)
=
\prod_{u \in \lambda} \left(1- q^{1+\leg(u)}t^{\arm(u)}\right)
\sum_{\gamma \sim \lambda}
\frac{ \macdonaldE_{\gamma}(x_1,\dotsc,x_n;q^{-1},t^{-1})}{
\prod_{v \in \gamma} \left(1- q^{1+\leg(v)}t^{\arm(v)}\right)
}.
\end{equation*}
The polynomial $\macdonaldE_{\gamma}(\xvec;q,t)$
is a \hyperref[macdonaldE]{nonsymmetric Macdonald polynomial}.

The same formula can also be expressed using
\hyperref[macdonaldEperm]{permuted-basement Macdonald polynomials}:
\begin{equation*}
\macdonaldP_\lambda(\xvec;q,t)
=
\prod_{u \in \lambda} \left(1- q^{1+\leg(u)}t^{\arm(u)}\right)
\sum_{\gamma \sim \lambda}
\frac{ \macdonaldE^{id}_{\gamma}(x_1,\dotsc,x_n;q,t)}{
\prod_{v \in \gamma} \left(1- q^{1+\leg(v)}t^{\arm(v)}\right)
},
\end{equation*}
since we have the relation
$\macdonaldE^{id}_{\gamma}(x_1,\dotsc,x_n;q,t)
= \macdonaldE_{\gamma}(x_n,\dotsc,x_1;q^{-1},t^{-1})$.

A related expression from \cite{ColmenarejoRam2022x} is
\begin{equation*}
\macdonaldP_\lambda(\xvec;q,t) = \frac{1}{W_\lambda(t)}
\sum_{w \in \symS_n} w \left( \macdonaldE_{\gamma}(\xvec;q,t)
\prod_{i \lt j} \frac{x_i - t x_j}{x_i - x_j}
\right),
\end{equation*}
where $W_\lambda(t)$ is a normalization constant, so that
$[x^{\lambda}] \macdonaldP_\lambda =1$.
Compare this with the formula for
\hyperref[hallLittlewoodP]{Hall--Littlewood polynomials}, obtained as
$q\to 0$.

In fact, an analogue of this formula exists for any fixed basement,
see \cite[Thm.~29]{Alexandersson2015gbMacdonald}.

\name{Cristian Lenart}, \name{Satoshi Naito}, \name{Fumihiko Nomoto}, and
\name{Daisuke Sagaki} give a path model for symmetric and nonsymmetric
Macdonald polynomials of arbitrary type \cite{LenartNaitoNomotoSagaki2025}.
The model uses pseudo-quantum Lakshmibai--Seshadri paths and gives a
counterpart of the Ram--Yip formula with less data than alcove walks.  The
paths also carry a connected pseudo-crystal structure.

\subsection[macdonaldPProperties]{Specializations}

The Macdonald polynomials specialize to other families of symmetric functions:
\begin{align*}
\schurS_\lambda(\xvec) &= \macdonaldP_\lambda(\xvec;q,q), \\
\hallLittlewoodP_\lambda(\xvec;t) &= \macdonaldP_\lambda(\xvec;0,t), \\
\jackP_\lambda(\xvec;a) &= \lim_{t \to 1} \macdonaldP_\lambda(\xvec;t^a,t).
\end{align*}

By rescaling, the
\defin{integral form Macdonald polynomials}\label{integralFormMacdonaldPolynomial}
are defined via
\begin{equation*}
\macdonaldJ_\lambda(\xvec;q,t)
=
\left(
  \prod_{\square \in \lambda} 1-q^{\arm(\square)}t^{1+\leg(\square)}
\right)
\macdonaldP_\lambda(\xvec;q,t).
\end{equation*}

Similarly, the integral-form Jack polynomials are obtained as a limit:
\[
\jackJ_\lambda(\xvec;a)
=
\lim_{t \to 1}
\frac{\macdonaldJ_\lambda(\xvec;t^a,t)}{(1-t)^{|\lambda|}}
\]

The \hyperref[macdonaldH]{modified Macdonald polynomials}
(also known as transformed Macdonald polynomials) are obtained by letting
\begin{equation*}
\macdonaldH_\lambda(\xvec;q,t) \coloneqq
t^{n(\lambda)}H_\lambda(\xvec;q,1/t),
\qquad
H_\lambda(\xvec;q,t) = \macdonaldJ_\lambda[\xvec/(1-t);q,t],
\end{equation*}
and we use \hyperref[plethysm]{plethystic notation} in the last expression.


\name{Emma Yu Jin} and \name{Xiaowei Lin} unify several combinatorial
formulas and generalize them to superized versions \cite{JinLin2026x}.
In particular, their formula specializes to the formulas by
Corteel--Mandelshtam--Williams \cite{CorteelMandelshtamWilliams2018x} and
Corteel--Mandelshtam--Mason--Williams \cite{CorteelMandelshtamMasonWilliams2022}.



\subsection[macdonaldJqtKostka]{$qt$-Kostka polynomials}

Macdonald \cite[p.~354]{Macdonald1987} introduces the
\defin{$qt$-Kostka polynomials}\label{qtKostkaPolynomial}
$K_{\lambda\mu}(q,t)$
via the relation
\[
\macdonaldJ_\mu(\xvec;q,t)
=
\sum_{\lambda} K_{\lambda\mu}(q,t) \schurS_\lambda[\xvec(1-t)]
\]
where
\[
\schurS_\lambda[\xvec(1-t)]
=
\sum_{\rho} \frac{\chi_\rho^\lambda}{z_\rho} \powerSum_\rho(x)
\prod_{i=1}^{\length(\rho)} (1-t^{\rho_i}).
\]
Macdonald conjectured that the $K_{\lambda\mu}(q,t)$ are polynomials in
$\setN[q,t]$ \cite{Macdonald1987}.
\name{Mark Haiman} later confirmed this \cite{Haiman2001}, as the property
follows from \hyperref[macdonaldHSchurPositivity]{the Schur positivity of the
modified Macdonald polynomials},
and the corresponding modified $qt$-Kostka polynomials.

It is still an open problem to find a combinatorial interpretation of
$K_{\lambda\mu}(q,t)$.

\name{Konstantin Matveev} and \name{Yuchen Wei} prove a factorization formula
for certain
\defin{Macdonald Littlewood--Richardson coefficients}\label{macdonaldLittlewoodRichardsonCoefficient}
$c^\nu_{\lambda\mu}(q,t)$ \cite{MatveevWei2023x}.  Their result applies when
the corresponding Kostka number $K_{\mu,\nu-\lambda}$ is equal to $1$, giving a
Macdonald analogue of a special case of a factorization conjecture of
\name{Richard Stanley}.



\subsection[macdonaldJHaglundsConjecture]{Haglund's conjecture}

\name{Jim Haglund} formulates
\defin{Haglund's Macdonald conjecture}\label{haglundMacdonaldConjecture},
in \cite{Haglund2010}:
\[
\left\langle
  \frac{\macdonaldJ_\lambda(\xvec;q,q^k)}{(1-q)^{|\lambda|}},
  \schurS_\mu(\xvec)
\right\rangle
\in \setN[q]
\]
for any $k \in 0,1,2,\dotsc$.
\name{Jim Haglund}, \name{Mark Haiman}, and \name{Nicholas Loehr} prove that
the expression
$\frac{\macdonaldJ_\lambda(\xvec;q,q^k)}{(1-q)^{|\lambda|}}$
has coefficients in $\setN[q]$ when expanded in the $\monomial_\mu$-basis
\cite{HaglundHaimanLoehr2005}.
Haglund's conjecture states that the expression is actually
\hyperref[schurPositiveDefinition]{Schur-positive}.

\name{Arun Ram} suggests that Haglund's conjecture can be made stronger:
a positivity statement should hold when $\macdonaldJ_\lambda(\xvec;q,q^k)$
is expanded in the $\macdonaldJ_\lambda(\xvec;q,q^{k-1})$-basis.  Note that
$\macdonaldJ_\lambda(\xvec;q,q)=\schurS_\lambda$.

Haglund's conjecture has been proved in some special cases; see
\cite{Yoo2012,Yoo2015,Bhattacharya2022x}.



\name{Michel Lassalle} formulates conjectures for Macdonald polynomials of
types $B$, $C$, and $D$ \cite{Lassalle2005x}.

A \hyperref[quasiSymmetricFunctions]{quasisymmetric} refinement of
$\macdonaldJ_\lambda(\xvec;q,t)$
is introduced in \cite{CorteelMandelshtamMasonWilliams2022}.
The \defin{quasisymmetric Macdonald polynomials}\label{quasisymmetricMacdonaldPolynomial}
$G_{\alpha}(\xvec;q,t)$
are defined as sums over certain nonsymmetric Macdonald polynomials,
such that the following properties hold:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\macdonaldJ_\lambda(\xvec;q,t)$ is a positive sum of $G_{\alpha}(\xvec;q,t)$.
\item $G_{\alpha}(\xvec;q,t)$ is quasisymmetric.
\item $G_{\alpha}(\xvec;0,0)$ is a quasisymmetric Schur function.
\item The $G_{\alpha}(\xvec;q,t)$ have a combinatorial HHL-type formula.
\end{itemize}

\name{Camilo González} and \name{Luc Lapointe} introduce the
\defin{multi-Macdonald polynomials}\label{multiMacdonaldPolynomial},
which are indexed by $r$-tuples of
partitions \cite{GonzalesLapointe2019}.
They are defined via triangularity relations, similar to the definition of
\hyperref[macdonaldP]{Macdonald P polynomials}.
When $r=1$, the usual Macdonald $P$ polynomials are recovered, and when
$r=2$, the family coincides with the
\defin{double Macdonald polynomials}\label{doubleMacdonaldPolynomial},
previously defined in \cite{BlondeauFournierLapointeMathieu2014}.

The multi-Macdonald polynomials can be expressed as a product of Macdonald
$P$ polynomials,
with suitable plethystic substitutions in each factor.

The same construction has an analogue of the
\hyperref[macdonaldH]{modified Macdonald polynomials}, and then an analogue
of the $qt$-Kostka polynomials when expanded in the
\hyperref[schurWreath]{Wreath product Schur polynomials}.
González and Lapointe show that the multi-$qt$-Kostka polynomials are in
$\setN[q,t]$.

  Rating   & 1 \\

\end{polydata}

The \defin{wreath Macdonald $P$ polynomials}\label{wreathMacdonaldPolynomial},
$\macdonaldP_{\lambdavec^\bullet}(\xvec;q,t)$,
were conjectured to exist in \name{Mark Haiman's} seminal article
\cite[Sec.~7.2.19]{Haiman2002hilbertSchemes}.
This conjecture was later proved in \cite{BezrukavnikovFinkelbergVologodsky2014}.
See \cite{OrrShimozono2023x} for a survey on wreath Macdonald P polynomials.

\name{Joshua Jeishing Wen} realizes wreath Macdonald polynomials as
eigenstates in the vertex representation of a quantum toroidal algebra
\cite{Wen2025}.  In this model they diagonalize the horizontal Heisenberg
subalgebra, and the proof also gives another construction of the wreath
Macdonald polynomials using shuffle algebra methods.

\name{Dario Mathiä} and \name{Ulrich Thiel} show that wreath Macdonald
polynomials specialized at $q=t$ give characters of simple modules for
restricted rational Cherednik algebras of wreath product groups
\cite{MathiaThiel2022}.  This generalizes Gordon's character formula for the
symmetric group.

\name{Daniel Orr} and \name{Mark Shimozono} give explicit $q$-difference
operators acting diagonally on wreath Macdonald $P$-polynomials
\cite{OrrShimozono2024}.  This gives a finite-variable operator model for
the diagonalization property.
\name{Daniel Orr}, \name{Mark Shimozono}, and \name{Joshua Wen} construct
difference-permutation operators diagonalized by wreath Macdonald
$P$-polynomials \cite{OrrShimozonoWen2025}.  Their eigenvalues are elementary
symmetric polynomials of arbitrary degree, and the operators come from integral
formulas in the vertex representation of the corresponding quantum toroidal
algebra.

\name{Marino Romero} and \name{Joshua Jeishing Wen} prove Tesler identities
for wreath Macdonald polynomials \cite{RomeroWen2025x}.  Their operator
formula sends a wreath Macdonald polynomial to a delta function attached to
its character, leading to reciprocity results,
\defin{wreath interpolation Macdonald polynomials}\label{wreathInterpolationMacdonaldPolynomial},
plethystic formulas for wreath $(q,t)$-Kostka coefficients, and
series solutions to the corresponding bispectral problem.
\name{Seamus Albion Ferlinc} and \name{Joshua Jeishing Wen} introduce
Ext-operator analogues of the Carlsson--Nekrasov--Okounkov vertex operator
for wreath Macdonald polynomials \cite{FerlincWen2025x}.  As an application
they prove a modular $(q,t)$-Nekrasov--Okounkov formula for $r\geq 3$.
